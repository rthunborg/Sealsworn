# Game Brief: Sealsworn

**Date:** 2026-05-31
**Author:** Rasmus
**Status:** Draft for GDD Development

---

## Executive Summary

Sealsworn is a turn-based dark fantasy roguelite where oathbound descendants descend a shifting containment Labyrinth, forging deadly builds from awakened memories to stop an immortal horror from breaking free.

**Target Audience:** Core roguelite and RPG players, roughly ages 18-40, who enjoy tactical choices, build synergies, loot decisions, repeated runs, and meaningful progression. Secondary audiences include dark fantasy lore explorers, tactics players seeking shorter runs, RPG buildcrafters, and mobile players who want depth without real-time pressure.

**Core Pillars:** Think Forever, Act Carefully; Builds Bend the Rules; The Labyrinth Is a Tactical Place; Mystery Rewards Replay.

**Key Differentiators:** Mobile-first tactical roguelite depth, weapon-shaped combat in a single-hero roguelite, awakened-memory passives with Consume/Destroy choices, and a forward-sealing Labyrinth that makes every route a commitment.

**Platform:** iOS and Android mobile/tablet with Windows desktop/laptop as a priority target. Console and web are out of scope; Mac and Linux may be considered later.

**Success Vision:** Sealsworn succeeds when players finish or lose a run and want one more descent because they made interesting tactical choices, shaped a build that changed how they played, faced tempting Labyrinth risks, and remembered a meaningful discovery or decision.

## Game Vision

### Core Concept

A turn-based dark fantasy roguelite where oathbound descendants descend a shifting containment labyrinth, forging deadly builds from awakened memories to stop an immortal horror from breaking free.

### Elevator Pitch

Sealsworn is a mobile-first, turn-based dark fantasy roguelite where every move matters as you descend a shifting Labyrinth built to contain an immortal cosmic horror. Choose a hero, position carefully, and shape each run through weapon-driven attacks, dangerous route choices, loot, and awakened-memory passives that bend the rules of combat. Death returns your spirit to the last outpost, where recovered memories unlock new possibilities for the next descent.

### Vision Statement

Sealsworn should feel like a tactical roguelite where every run becomes its own dark little legend: a risky build, a strange discovery, a near disaster, and one more piece of a forgotten war remembered. Players should put the game down thinking about the build that almost came together, the secret they uncovered, or the one decision that changed everything, then feel pulled back for another descent.

Sealsworn is designed for core roguelite and RPG players, roughly ages 18-40, who enjoy tactical choices, build synergies, loot decisions, and repeated runs where knowledge matters. They are comfortable with roguelites, turn-based games, or tactics games, but the game should remain readable enough for curious RPG players who are newer to the genre. The ideal player wants mobile-friendly sessions without giving up depth, mystery, or long-term progression.

**Demographics:**

Players roughly ages 18-40, skewing toward core PC and mobile players who already engage with roguelites, tactical games, RPGs, or premium-style mobile games.

**Gaming Preferences:**

They prefer turn-based or decision-heavy games where planning matters more than reflexes. They enjoy buildcraft, run variety, meaningful loot choices, tactical positioning, fair-but-tense risk, meta progression, and systems that reward learning over time.

**Motivations:**

They are drawn by the promise of clever synergies, tense decisions, dangerous runs, satisfying progression, and discoveries that make each attempt feel different. They want to feel smart for surviving, adapting, and making a build work under pressure.

Secondary audiences include dark fantasy lore explorers, tactics players seeking shorter runs, RPG buildcrafters, and mobile players who want meaningful depth without real-time pressure.

Dark fantasy and lore-discovery players may be drawn to the Labyrinth, the forgotten order, awakened memories, relics, environmental storytelling, codex entries, and optional cosmic horror mystery. They need the game to be readable and not overly punishing at the start.

Tactical and strategy players may respond to positioning, weapon-shaped attacks, fog of war, enemy pattern learning, fair information, and turn-based play with no real-time pressure. They need clear rules and satisfying board-state decisions without the commitment of a long tactics campaign.

RPG buildcrafters and loot fans may care most about class identity, passives, equipment, consume/destroy choices, loot pivots, and run-specific stories where a build becomes strange or powerful.

Mobile players seeking depth need clean UI, readable sessions, saves between levels, interruption-friendly pacing, and a presentation that clearly distinguishes Sealsworn from idle or filler mobile games.

**Market Opportunity:**

The opportunity is a mobile-first, spatial tactical roguelite RPG with loot, positioning, weapon-shaped attacks, and build-bending passives. Many successful roguelites are deckbuilders, dice games, action games, or desktop-first tactics games. Sealsworn can offer the depth and tension of a tactical roguelite in shorter, interruption-friendly sessions, wrapped in dark medieval fantasy and optional cosmic mystery.

Sealsworn will compete for attention with deep mobile roguelikes such as Shattered Pixel Dungeon, buildcraft roguelites such as Slay the Spire, Monster Train, Balatro, and Dawncaster, tactical roguelites such as Into the Breach, Slice & Dice, Dicey Dungeons, and Hoplite, and dark fantasy RPGs such as Darkest Dungeon.

Discoverability should lean on both PC and mobile channels: Steam demos, Steam Next Fest, roguelite and tactics creators, short clips of strange builds and risky decisions, seeded-run sharing, and mobile store visibility through clear screenshots, short gameplay videos, readable phone-sized UI, strong icon art, and keywords such as turn-based roguelite, dark fantasy RPG, tactical dungeon crawler, and offline RPG.

## Game Fundamentals

### Core Gameplay Pillars

**Think Forever, Act Carefully**

The player should never feel rushed by real-world time, but every committed action should matter. Movement, attacks, waiting, routing, and ability use all advance danger, so tension comes from consequences rather than reflex pressure.

**Builds Bend the Rules**

Weapons, loot, passives, affinities, and route choices should create runs that feel meaningfully different. The best moments come when the player feels clever for turning a strange reward or risky choice into a powerful synergy.

**The Labyrinth Is a Tactical Place**

Positioning, line of sight, fog of war, hazards, doors, enemy movement, level size, and forward-only commitment should make space matter. The game should not become abstract stat trading; where the hero stands and which path they choose must stay important.

**Mystery Rewards Replay**

Story, secrets, enemy knowledge, passive flavor, affinities, and meta progression should unfold across runs without becoming required reading. The player should feel that each descent might reveal something new mechanically, narratively, or strategically.

**Pillar Priority:** When pillars conflict, prioritize:

Gameplay clarity wins moment-to-moment; build excitement wins run-to-run; mystery wins long-term. If a feature damages tactical readability, it should be simplified. If it makes builds less exciting, it should be reworked. If it over-explains the world or kills discovery, it should be held back.

### Primary Mechanics

**Core Turn Verbs**

Players move across the grid with a limited movement budget, attack using the shape and rules of the equipped weapon, wait or reposition when timing matters, use class tools, consumables, or special effects when available, and commit each action knowing enemies and level systems advance afterward. These are the most repeated actions and must feel clear, readable, and tactically meaningful.

**Run-Building Verbs**

Players choose the next node on the forward-only run map, equip weapons, armor, jewelry, and support items, compare loot, consume awakened-memory passives to gain rule-bending effects, destroy passives to reject them for alternate benefits or hidden outcomes, and risk HP, curses, gold, or future safety for stronger rewards. These choices create the most satisfying run moments: finding a synergy, taking a dangerous bargain, or realizing a build has started to work.

**Exploration and Progression Verbs**

Players scout limited information about upcoming nodes, enter procedurally generated levels with visible size and partial unknowns, exit forward while accepting that missed opportunities are gone, discover enemies, affinities, passives, secrets, and story fragments, remember through meta progression at the last outpost, and unlock new classes, passives, items, enemies, affinities, and starting options.

**Core Loop:** Choose a path -> enter a level -> move, attack, and commit through tactical turns -> collect loot or passives -> shape the build -> exit forward -> remember or die -> unlock new possibilities.

### Player Experience Goals

Sealsworn should leave players feeling that they survived, learned, and almost certainly could do better next time. After a good session, the player should remember a tense tactical escape, a strange passive choice, a build that nearly paid off, or a mystery fragment that made them want one more descent.

**Core Emotional Targets:**

- Tense but fair
- Clever, not rushed
- Lucky but self-authored
- Mysterious, not confusing
- Dangerous but readable
- Replayable because each run teaches or reveals something

**Emotional Journey:** A run should move through caution, temptation, cleverness, tension, and relief or ruin. The player begins with limited knowledge and makes careful early choices. Loot, passives, routes, curses, and risky rewards then offer dangerous possibilities. As synergies emerge, the player feels ownership over the build. Once the run becomes valuable, every mistake feels sharper. Success should feel earned, while failure leaves behind knowledge, memory, and the urge to try again.

The experience is meaningful because the player is not just grinding numbers. They are mastering a tactical space, discovering strange build interactions, and slowly remembering a forgotten war beneath the world.

## Scope and Constraints

### Target Platforms

**Primary:** Mobile and tablet on iOS and Android, with Windows desktop/laptop support as a priority target.

**Secondary:** Possible future Mac and Linux support.

**Not Targeted:** Console and web are explicitly out of scope.

### Development Timeline

No fixed deadline. Development should proceed through validation milestones rather than date-driven commitments: first prove the smallest tactical loop, then weapon identity, procedural level quality, passive/buildcraft excitement, full run structure, meta progression, and finally content expansion.

### Budget Considerations

Sealsworn is a self-funded solo project supported by AI agents and tools. There is no fixed outsourcing budget, and paid outsourcing is not planned. Free and paid AI-assisted tools may be used across development, design, code, art, audio, writing, QA, and production support.

The main budget constraints are not direct cash spend, but production bandwidth, polish capacity, content volume, asset consistency, testing effort, and the risk of scope sprawl. Marketing effort is expected to stay minimal, so discoverability should rely on a clear store presence, readable visuals, compelling screenshots, strong short-form gameplay clips if available, and the game's core hook rather than a large campaign.

### Team Resources

The project is solo-led by an experienced software engineer and architect, supported by AI agents and tools across all roles. The project has no deadline pressure, allowing exploration and iteration, but scope should still be managed carefully because design, implementation, content creation, testing, UI polish, balancing, and release work ultimately depend on one human owner.

**Skill Gaps:** The largest risks are likely game-specific production experience, content balancing, mobile-first UX polish, cohesive art/audio direction, game feel, QA coverage across devices, and release/store operations. These should be treated as areas for deliberate tooling, prototypes, and validation rather than assumptions.

### Technical Constraints

The engine and technical stack are still open and need to be decided. The project should support mobile, tablet, and Windows desktop/laptop play from the architecture stage, with touch-first controls that also work well on desktop inputs.

MVP should not require multiplayer, accounts, leaderboards, cloud saves, daily challenges, or live services. Future iterations may consider leaderboards, cloud saves, daily challenges, seeded challenges, or other online-adjacent features after the core offline loop is proven.

Accessibility support is required to align with current industry expectations. The design should account for readable mobile UI, scalable text, clear iconography, colorblind-safe information design, low-reflex turn-based play, input clarity, skippable narrative/control-loss moments, and robust save/resume behavior.

### Scope Realities

The MVP should prove the complete roguelite loop before expanding content volume: start a run, choose a class, traverse a seeded forward-only map, enter tactical procedural levels, fight enemies, collect loot, make passive choices, visit simple risk/reward nodes, die or reach the finale, return to the outpost, and unlock limited meta progression.

Because the project is solo-led, the brief should favor systems that generate replayable variety from a manageable amount of content. Early production should prioritize tactical readability, mobile UI, procedural level quality, a small class set, a focused enemy roster, a limited passive pool, and strong build synergies over broad content volume.

## Reference Framework

### Inspiration Games

**Shattered Pixel Dungeon**

- Taking: Turn-based procedural dungeon play, generated maps, item drops, replayable mastery, and talent-like progression.
- Not Taking: Pixel-art nostalgia as a core identity, traditional roguelike friction as a goal, the attribute system, the hunger system, or any pressure that makes taking turns feel like a real-time resource.

**The Binding of Isaac**

- Taking: Passive synergies, rule-bending effects, secrets, unlocks, and the feeling that a run can become strange in memorable ways.
- Not Taking: Real-time action gameplay, small room-based combat structure, bullet-hell pressure, or its fiction and tone.

**Slay the Spire**

- Taking: Map nodes, route choice, visible risk/reward structure, run planning, and the clarity with which future choices shape the current build.
- Not Taking: Deckbuilding as the combat core.

### Competitive Analysis

**Direct Competitors:**

Sealsworn competes in a crowded broad roguelite market, but occupies a more specific underserved corner: mobile-friendly, turn-based tactical dungeon runs with RPG loot, spatial positioning, build synergies, and dark fantasy mystery.

Close competitors and reference competitors include Shattered Pixel Dungeon, Slice & Dice, Hoplite, Into the Breach, Slay the Spire, The Binding of Isaac, Darkest Dungeon, Dawncaster, and other mobile deckbuilders or run-based RPGs.

**Competitor Strengths:**

Shattered Pixel Dungeon offers depth, replayability, traditional roguelike mastery, and strong mobile turn-based dungeon play. Slice & Dice provides compact tactical decisions, build variety, and mobile-to-PC viability. Hoplite shows how elegant mobile turn-based tactics can make every move matter. Into the Breach demonstrates tactical clarity, fair consequence-driven turns, and readable board states.

Slay the Spire excels at route clarity, build direction, reward evaluation, and run mastery. The Binding of Isaac is a benchmark for weird passive synergies, secrets, unlocks, and memorable run mutations. Darkest Dungeon competes on dark fantasy atmosphere, expedition pressure, and costly decisions. Dawncaster and similar mobile deckbuilders prove that there is an audience for deeper premium run-based RPG systems on phones.

**Competitor Weaknesses:**

Traditional mobile roguelikes can feel visually nostalgic, opaque, or friction-heavy for modern players. Some tactical roguelites are elegant but narrow in RPG progression or long-term build variety. Deckbuilders and dice games often deliver strong buildcraft but lack spatial dungeon exploration and positioning. Action roguelites can create build excitement but rely on reflex pressure that Sealsworn intentionally avoids. Dark fantasy expedition games can become campaign-management or stress-management experiences rather than compact tactical runs.

### Key Differentiators

**Mobile-First Tactical Roguelite Depth**

Sealsworn is designed around thoughtful, interruption-friendly turn-based play from the start, not as a simplified mobile port. It gives mobile players real tactical depth, readable grid combat, meaningful positioning, and run progression without real-time pressure.

**Weapon-Shaped Combat in a Single-Hero Roguelite**

The player's basic attack changes meaningfully based on equipped weapon: melee reach, ranged penalties, staff projectiles, wands ignoring blockers, shields and tomes as support tools, and other equipment-defined combat rules. This makes loot affect tactical decisions directly, not just stats.

**Awakened-Memory Passives with Consume/Destroy Choices**

Passives are not just buffs. They are recovered memories, oaths, scars, relic echoes, and broken protocols that can bend rules, create synergies, or introduce risk. The choice to consume or destroy them gives the player a recurring build-defining decision with both mechanical and story weight.

**A Forward-Sealing Labyrinth That Makes Every Route a Commitment**

Levels and map choices are forward-only. The player can scout limited information, choose a path, and then live with the consequences. This creates tension, replayability, and a strong identity: every descent is a chain of commitments into a prison built to contain something worse than death.

**Unique Value Proposition:**

Sealsworn stands out by combining mobile-first tactical clarity, weapon-driven spatial combat, rule-bending memory passives, and a forward-only containment Labyrinth where every route choice becomes part of the run's story.

## Content Framework

### World and Setting

Sealsworn takes place in a dark medieval fantasy world where ordinary civilization is defined by stone keeps, old roads, village superstition, steel weapons, candlelit shrines, ruined chapels, and forgotten bloodlines. Beneath that familiar fantasy surface sits something far older and stranger: the Labyrinth, a shifting containment structure built around the only known breach point through which an immortal cosmic horror can enter the world.

The world-building should be evocative but restrained. The essential premise is simple: the seals are broken, something inside is waking, and the descendants of the forgotten Wardens are bound to descend. Deeper truths should unfold through optional fragments: passive names, codex entries, enemy descriptions, boss reveals, outpost changes, and strange details inside the Labyrinth.

The atmosphere should be tense, ancient, mournful, and uncanny. The world should feel like it is remembering a duty too late: medieval fantasy at the surface, cosmic horror in the cracks, and forgotten Wardenwork beneath the stone.

### Narrative Approach

Sealsworn should use a story-light, lore-rich, discovery-driven narrative approach. The main game loop must work for players who skip every line of text, but players who care should feel rewarded for piecing together the Labyrinth's purpose, the cult's beliefs, the old order's failures, and the true nature of the being beneath the world.

**Story Delivery:** Story should come through a short opening premise, passive and item flavor, optional Codex or Archive entries, environmental details, boss reveals, run outcomes, outpost changes, and emergent run stories. Control loss should be minimal and always skippable. Any intro, death moment, boss reveal, or post-victory twist should be brief and skippable.

A dedicated narrative workflow will likely be useful later, but not as a full lore bible yet. The first narrative pass should define tone, naming rules, passive flavor patterns, boss reveal lines, codex style, and the minimum story beats needed for MVP.

### Content Volume

MVP content should be intentionally narrow and focused on proving the complete roguelite loop. Recommended MVP boundaries include 3 playable classes, 3 enemy families, 3-4 affinities, 20-30 passives, a small item pool, simple shops or risk/reward nodes, one major finale, and enough secrets or codex entries to prove the discovery layer without building a full lore library.

## Art and Audio Direction

### Visual Style

Sealsworn should use a stylized dark fantasy 2.5D look built for mobile clarity first. The camera should be top-down or slightly angled orthographic grid, comparable in readability to Shattered Pixel Dungeon, with readable tiles, strong silhouettes, clear enemy shapes, and effects that communicate danger without cluttering the board.

The world should feel medieval and decayed: stone, iron, bone, ash, wax, parchment, blood, ruined masonry, old shrines, and rare black-glass or rune-metal Wardenwork. The palette should be dark and grounded with controlled accents: candle amber, seal-blue, blood red, sickly green, pale bone, and void-black. The style should avoid pixel nostalgia, photorealism, sci-fi neon, and art so dark that the tactical board becomes hard to read.

Animation should be simple but expressive: readable attacks, enemy movement, impact flashes, passive pickup pulses, seal cracks, and soul-return effects. MVP does not need elaborate cutscenes or complex character animation.

**References:** Shattered Pixel Dungeon for camera/readability rather than pixel style; dark medieval fantasy and cosmic horror references for mood; Sealsworn's own Wardenwork language for relic-magic accents.

### Audio Style

Music should be sparse, mournful, ritual, and tense rather than constantly epic. The sound palette should include low drones, distant choirs, slow percussion, bowed strings, bells, stone resonance, and occasional silence. The Labyrinth should feel ancient and alive without overwhelming tactical thinking.

Sound effects should be crisp and satisfying: weapon hits, armor impacts, footsteps, doors sealing, relic hums, curses, broken protocols, passive pickups, and UI confirmations. Horror ambience should stay subtle through whispers, distant movement, low rumbles, and unnatural tones.

Voice should be minimal or absent for MVP. If used at all, it should be limited to nonverbal whispers, breaths, chants, or short stingers.

### Production Approach

For a solo, AI-assisted build, the safest path is a cohesive stylized art direction with modular assets: reusable tile sets, enemy silhouettes, item and passive icons, UI frames, and a small number of strong background or outpost illustrations. AI tools can support concept art, icons, mood boards, passive illustrations, and texture ideas. Asset stores are acceptable for placeholder or final sound effects, ambience, UI sounds, and possibly modular environment pieces.

Avoid production-heavy commitments such as bespoke cutscenes, large animated character rosters, fully hand-painted unique art for every room, realistic 3D, or a visual style where every asset must be custom-made to look coherent.

### Technical Challenges

The main technical challenges are selecting the right engine/stack, building mobile-first tactical UI, ensuring save/resume reliability, keeping seeded generation deterministic, validating procedural levels, making weapon-shaped attacks readable, and supporting data-driven content for future balancing.

### Market Risks

Sealsworn enters a crowded roguelite market and must avoid looking like either a generic mobile dungeon crawler or another deckbuilder-adjacent roguelite. Its market positioning depends on communicating tactical depth, premium feel, weapon-driven spatial combat, and the forward-sealing Labyrinth hook quickly through store assets, demos, and short gameplay clips.

### Mitigation Strategies

The main mitigation is sequencing: prove the smallest fun tactical loop first, then weapon identity, then procedural levels, then passives/buildcraft, then full run/meta progression. Development should prioritize mobile readability, tactical clarity, generator validation, data-driven tools, and narrow MVP content before expanding classes, enemies, affinities, secrets, or narrative volume.

## Success Criteria

### MVP Definition

The MVP should prove one complete, replayable roguelite loop: choose a hero, enter a seeded forward-only Labyrinth run, survive tactical turn-based levels, shape a build through loot and awakened-memory passives, die or defeat a first finale boss, then return to the last outpost with some retained progress.

**Required Features:**

- Start new run.
- Choose from 3 classes: Warrior, Pyromancer, Ranger.
- Generate a seeded forward-only run map.
- Show 2-3 next-node choices with at least level size visible.
- Enter procedural Small/Medium levels, with Large optional only if manageable.
- Support grid movement, fog of war, line of sight, HP, armor/dodge placeholders, enemy turns, and basic enemy AI.
- Implement weapon-shaped basic attacks for a small weapon set: sword, spear, bow, staff, wand, plus shield/tome as support if feasible.
- Include loot drops, simple equipment, small inventory, gold, and at least one gold sink such as shop or reforge.
- Include 20-30 passives with Consume/Destroy choices, including 3-5 rule-benders.
- Include 3 enemy families: Cultists, Undead, Demons, with a few roles each.
- Include 3-4 affinities: Scorched, Flooded/Conductive, Cursed, and Darkness or Frozen.
- Include one finale: Larval Avatar or equivalent first boss.
- Include death, victory, run summary, and return to the last outpost.
- Include basic meta progression using Oath Shards or equivalent, focused on unlocking variety rather than raw stat grind.
- Include save/resume between levels and seed visibility for debugging.

**Minimum Content to Prove the Concept:**

- 1 short opening paragraph.
- 1 last-outpost screen/menu.
- 3 playable classes.
- 8-12 selected nodes per full run, adjustable after testing.
- 2-3 level sizes.
- 1-2 special node types, such as shop/reforge/passive reward.
- 20-30 passives.
- Small but expressive loot pool.
- 8-12 enemy types total across the 3 families.
- 1 boss.
- Basic codex/archive as placeholder or very light content.

**Cut or Add Later:**

Necromancer, Shadeblade, Huge levels, deep class mastery trees, large item/affix pools, full boss roster, full lore bible, complex secret chains, advanced elemental interaction matrix, complex AI intent UI, deep outpost exploration, voice acting, and elaborate cutscenes.

**MVP Success Test:**

A player should be able to complete or die in a full seeded run and feel: "I made interesting tactical choices, my build changed how I played, the Labyrinth offered tempting risks, and I want to try another descent."

### Success Metrics

For the MVP or vertical slice, the most important success metric is not revenue yet. It is whether players want another run.

| Category | Metric | How to Measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Acquisition | Steam wishlists, demo downloads, mobile store page visits | Steamworks, app store analytics, landing page analytics |
| Activation | % of players who start a run after opening the game | In-game analytics |
| Engagement | Runs per player | Track runs started per session and across days |
| Replay Pull | % of players who start another run after death or victory | In-game analytics |
| Session Fit | Average session length and level completion time | Track time per level, per run, and before quit/resume |
| Progression | % of players unlocking first meta upgrade, class unlock, or passive discovery | In-game progression events |
| Run Quality | % of runs with at least one passive choice, loot decision, and meaningful route choice | Instrument key run events |
| Completion | % reaching first boss / defeating first boss | In-game analytics |
| Retention | Day 1 / Day 7 return rate for demo or test build | Platform analytics or telemetry |
| Quality | Crash rate, save failures, generator failures, bug reports | Error logging, tester reports, seeded bug reports |
| Satisfaction | Player survey responses after playtest | Ask "Did you want to start another run?" and "What moment do you remember?" |
| Community | Discord joins, mailing list signups, creator interest, fan posts | Community/platform tracking |
| Financial | Sales, conversion from demo to purchase, revenue per platform | Steam/app store sales dashboards |

**Most Important Early Success Signals:**

- Players start a second run after dying.
- Players can describe a build or passive choice they cared about.
- Players understand why they won or lost.
- Players feel the mobile-first UI is readable and comfortable.
- Players report at least one "I want to see what happens if..." moment.

**North Star Metric:** Replay intent: the percentage of players who start another run after a meaningful death, victory, or unlock. If players consistently want one more descent, Sealsworn is working.

### Launch Goals

Launch goals should remain secondary until the core loop is proven. The likely path is a polished MVP or vertical slice, then a Steam demo or test build to validate replay intent, readability, and market positioning before broader mobile release planning.

Commercial goals should focus on converting demonstrated interest into wishlists, demo plays, creator coverage, and eventual sales rather than assuming a large marketing campaign.

5. Prototype passive Consume/Destroy choices with 8-10 rough awakened-memory passives. Test whether players understand the choice and whether it creates build excitement.
6. Test procedural level generation with Small and Medium levels, validating entrance/exit reachability, enemy placement, optional branches, rewards, and forward-only commitment.
7. Create an art/UI style test with one small board, one hero, 2-3 enemies, a passive modal, and a last-outpost/menu mockup.
8. Run early playtests with roguelite/RPG/tactics players and ask whether they wanted another run, whether they understood why they won or lost, and what moment they remembered.

### Research Needs

Research and validation should focus on engine/tech stack fit, mobile-first tactical UI conventions, seed determinism boundaries, procedural level generation quality, save/resume architecture, accessibility requirements, and how comparable premium mobile/PC roguelites communicate depth through screenshots, clips, and demos.

### Open Questions

1. Which engine or tech stack best supports mobile-first tactical roguelite development?
2. Should the first build target desktop, mobile, or both?
3. Should the fourth MVP affinity be Darkness or Frozen?
4. How embodied does the last outpost need to be for MVP?
5. How deterministic should drops and combat events be beyond map/level seeds?
6. What is the minimum passive pool that creates build excitement?
7. What is the smallest playable loop that proves: a player can move, fight, choose loot/passives, exit forward, die, and want another descent?
