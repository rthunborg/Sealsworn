## Technique Execution Results

**First Principles Thinking:**

**Interactive Focus:** Define the essential feel of a satisfying mobile-first fantasy roguelite run before adding inherited RPG assumptions.

**Key Ideas Generated:**

**[Core Loop #1]**: Variable-Length Synergy Hunt
_Concept_: A full run usually lasts around 10-60 minutes, while failed runs can end very quickly and especially thoughtful or successful runs can exceed 60 minutes. Any run longer than a few minutes should grant meta progression, so even failed attempts contribute to the broader game.
_Novelty_: The emotional center is not just "clear rooms and get stronger," but the tense hope of surviving long enough to find the missing skill, item, or loot piece that causes a build to snap into an overpowered state before the finale.

**[Combat Feel #2]**: Tactical Proximity Positioning
_Concept_: Positioning matters because distance and adjacency change combat outcomes: some attacks are stronger up close, safer at range, or modified by terrain and room shape. The player should often care where they stand before choosing what to do.
_Novelty_: This keeps combat spatial and mobile-readable without requiring real-time reflexes or a large tactical board.

**[Ability Design #3]**: Anti-Hoarding Ability Design
_Concept_: The game should avoid long cooldowns and consumables that players save forever. Abilities should encourage active use through short cycles, conditional refreshes, escalating bonuses, or costs that create interesting immediate tradeoffs.
_Novelty_: The player feels clever for using tools now, not guilty for failing to preserve them for an imagined future crisis.

**[Risk Reward #4]**: Curse Economy
_Concept_: Tempting power can be bought with real run costs: reduced maximum HP, cursed items, dangerous wheel-of-fortune events, unstable relics, or debt-like consequences that make the next few rooms scarier. These gambles should offer believable paths to broken synergies.
_Novelty_: Risk is not just "take damage for gold"; it becomes a build-shaping economy where the player buys volatility.

**[Progression #5]**: Strategic Build Soft Gate
_Concept_: A player can get reasonably far with general tactical play, but beating the game should usually require some coherent build choices. The game rewards paying attention to synergies without making every run feel solved from the start.
_Novelty_: Strategy matters as a soft gate instead of a hard knowledge check, preserving both accessibility and mastery.

**[Resource Model #6]**: HP-and-Turn Economy
_Concept_: The main pressure systems are HP and turns. Every movement, attack, or ability use advances enemies too, turning "time" into a tactical resource without imposing any real-world timer.
_Novelty_: The game can feel tense and urgent while still letting the player think indefinitely before acting.

**[Enemy Design #7]**: Enemy Pattern Literacy
_Concept_: Enemies do not generally reveal their next move, but learning their usual behaviors helps the player predict danger. Familiarity with enemy types becomes a skill layer across runs.
_Novelty_: The game rewards accumulated player knowledge without relying on explicit intent previews for every enemy action.

**[Enemy Design #8]**: Major Threat Telegraphs
_Concept_: Exceptionally dangerous or grand enemy abilities can be signaled one or more turns in advance, giving the player a chance to move, interrupt, counter, or prepare. These telegraphs become dramatic punctuation rather than the default combat language.
_Novelty_: The game preserves uncertainty in normal turns while still creating fair, memorable "oh no" moments.

**[Signature Hook #9]**: Room Manipulation Hook
_Concept_: Rooms themselves may become part of the tactical and build system: shifting tiles, rotating chambers, sealing doors, moving hazards, changing reward nodes, or altering room rules through abilities or relics. The room is not just a backdrop but a manipulable game object.
_Novelty_: This offers a distinctive identity beyond standard fantasy roguelite combat and could become the game's signature mechanic.

**[UX Principle #10]**: Think-Forever Turn Pressure
_Concept_: Turn count matters deeply, but real-world time never does. The player can stop and think for as long as they want, making the game comfortable for mobile interruptions while still tactically demanding once a move is committed.
_Novelty_: It separates strategic urgency from physical urgency, a crucial distinction for thoughtful mobile play.

**[Room System #11]**: Mutable Room Laws
_Concept_: Some rooms bend the usual game rules: fire might spread faster, healing might be reduced, movement could trigger hazards, or ranged attacks could weaken while adjacent attacks strengthen. These rule-bends create tactical puzzles and memorable run stories.
_Novelty_: The room becomes a temporary rules engine, not just a container for enemies and loot.

**[Map Flow #12]**: Forked Map Clues
_Concept_: As the player progresses, they choose the next room from a branching path, sometimes with partial clues about what lies ahead. Icons, rumors, colors, affixes, or discovered lore can hint at danger, reward type, room law, or enemy family.
_Novelty_: Path selection becomes a strategic decision with uncertainty, giving players a sense of reading the dungeon rather than following a linear sequence.

**[Reward Design #13]**: Odd Room Premiums
_Concept_: Strange or unusual rooms can offer special rewards if cleared, such as unusual relics, build-defining skills, rare crafting materials, or permanent run modifiers. The more unusual the room, the stronger the temptation.
_Novelty_: Weirdness itself becomes a reward signal, training the player to seek the uncanny.

**[Map Flow #14]**: Affix-To-Secret Chains
_Concept_: Rooms with special affixes can unlock hidden extra reward rooms if cleared under the affix's conditions. For example, clearing a "No Healing" room might reveal a blood shrine, while surviving a "Burning Floor" room might uncover a fire relic vault.
_Novelty_: Room affixes become doors to secret content, making hard modifiers feel like invitations rather than pure punishment.

**[Reward Design #15]**: Hidden Reward Rooms
_Concept_: The dungeon can contain bonus rooms that appear only through odd choices, affix clears, map clue interpretation, or risky sacrifices. These rooms become the player's best shot at finding the missing synergy piece.
_Novelty_: The path to overpowering a build is not only loot RNG, but also the player's willingness to interpret and chase suspicious opportunities.

**[Information Design #16]**: Partial Room Readability
_Concept_: The player can make strategic path decisions from partial information, such as room type, rough danger, theme, or reward hints, but never knows exactly what a room contains. This keeps planning meaningful while preserving suspense.
_Novelty_: The dungeon is legible enough to strategize against but never fully solved before entering.

**[Information Design #17]**: Paid Revelation
_Concept_: The player can sometimes spend a resource, accept a curse, lose max HP, or pay gold to reveal more information about future rooms. This turns information itself into a risk/reward purchase.
_Novelty_: Scouting becomes part of the same temptation economy as loot and power.

**[Build Path #18]**: Scout Build Path
_Concept_: Certain items, talents, classes, or relics reveal more information about future rooms, affixes, rewards, or hidden paths. A player can lean into foresight as a legitimate strategic advantage.
_Novelty_: Information gathering becomes a build identity rather than just a convenience feature.

**[Character Model #19]**: Single-Hero Class Foundation
_Concept_: The player controls one hero per run and chooses from a set of classes before starting. Classes provide fantasy identity, starting tools, and build direction while preserving a clean mobile-first control scheme.
_Novelty_: The game gets RPG class flavor without the UI and balance burden of a full party.

**[Class Design #20]**: Autonomous Summon Classes
_Concept_: Some classes can summon minions that are not directly controlled but help through predictable behavior, passive effects, blocking, attacks, scouting, sacrifice, or room interaction. The player makes strategic decisions around when and how to create or support them, not by micromanaging every minion turn.
_Novelty_: Summons add variety and fantasy texture while respecting the one-hero turn structure.

**[Class Design #21]**: Class Core With Run Mutations
_Concept_: Each class has a medium-strength identity: a recognizable playstyle, starting kit, and core fantasy, but each run can offer mutations, subclass-like choices, or build branches that significantly reshape how the class plays. Loot and room rewards can redirect a class without erasing what made it distinct.
_Novelty_: Classes feel meaningful without becoming fixed scripts, preserving roguelite discovery across repeated runs.

**[Class Design #22]**: Starter Kit Plus Oath
_Concept_: A run begins with a simple class kit, likely one basic attack, one class skill, and one passive, plus a small pre-run choice such as an oath, subclass, starter skill, or opening mutation. The class is readable immediately, while the pre-run choice gives repeat runs a different opening texture.
_Novelty_: It creates early agency without overloading the first screen with a complex build planner.

**[Gear Design #23]**: Gear-As-Starting-Flavor
_Concept_: Classes can start with different weapons, armor, or signature items that shape their initial tactical posture. A class might have a staff with safer range, a blade that rewards adjacency, or armor that changes how room hazards affect them.
_Novelty_: Starting gear communicates playstyle visually and mechanically, making class choice feel concrete from turn one.

**[Resource Design #24]**: Optional Class Resource
_Concept_: Class-specific resources such as mana, souls, rage, focus, or momentum remain an option, but only for classes that genuinely benefit from that extra layer. The baseline game should work through HP, turns, positioning, cooldown-light abilities, and loot synergies before adding universal resource complexity.
_Novelty_: Complexity is earned per class instead of imposed on every player action.

**[Level Design #25]**: Dynamic Levels
_Concept_: The game should think in terms of levels rather than only rooms. A level can change as the player moves through it: doors open or lock, hazards shift, rules mutate, enemies patrol, or rewards become reachable or lost.
_Novelty_: Procedural content becomes a changing tactical space rather than a chain of isolated combat boxes.

**[Level Design #26]**: Levels Contain Rooms
_Concept_: Each level may contain multiple rooms connected by doors, corridors, locks, events, hazards, and exits. Room-scale tactics still matter, but the player is also making level-scale decisions about routing and commitment.
_Novelty_: This gives the game a stronger dungeon-crawl feel while keeping each local encounter mobile-readable.

**[Risk Reward #27]**: Health-Breach Doors
_Concept_: Some locked or blocked doors can be forced open by sacrificing health, tackling through, breaking seals, or accepting injury. This gives players a risky way to chase rewards, shortcuts, or survival options even without the proper key.
_Novelty_: HP becomes a routing currency, not just a combat failure meter.

**[Class Design #28]**: Warrior Breach Passive
_Concept_: A Warrior-type class could have a passive that lets them force certain doors without spending health, or with reduced penalty. This makes physical toughness matter in exploration, not only combat.
_Novelty_: Class identity affects dungeon navigation and risk decisions, expanding class fantasy beyond damage patterns.

**[Level Design #29]**: Clear Level Exit
_Concept_: Every level should have a clear exit objective so the player understands how to progress. Even when the level contains optional paths, locked doors, odd rooms, and secret rewards, the main route forward remains readable.
_Novelty_: Procedural complexity stays approachable because the player always has a visible progression anchor.

**[Secret Design #30]**: Secret Exits
_Concept_: Some levels may contain hidden or alternate exits unlocked by clues, odd room conditions, keys, sacrifices, or special class interactions. These exits can lead to bonus levels, altered paths, rare rewards, or dangerous shortcuts.
_Novelty_: Exploration has mystery and long-tail discovery without requiring backtracking.

**[Map Rule #31]**: No-Backtracking Commitment
_Concept_: Once the player leaves a room or passes certain thresholds, they cannot backtrack to previous rooms. Each door choice becomes a commitment, and missed rewards or dangers become part of the run story.
_Novelty_: This turns navigation into forward momentum and prevents mobile sessions from bogging down in exhaustive map-clearing.

**First Principles Breakthroughs:**

- The core run fantasy is surviving long enough for a build to ignite into powerful synergy.
- The main pressure systems are HP, turn count, positioning, and irreversible level choices rather than real-time pressure.
- The dungeon should be partially readable but never fully certain.
- The game's signature identity is emerging around mutable level rules, forward-only commitment, risky doors, and hidden reward paths.
- Class identity should be medium-strength with run-specific mutations, preserving both fantasy and roguelite surprise.

**Morphological Analysis:**

**Interactive Focus:** Create a combinatorial idea grid across classes, level structures, room laws, risks, rewards, enemies, and build synergies.

**[Combination #32]**: Starved Necromancer Blood Door
_Concept_: In a Starved level where healing is scarce or max HP is pressured, the Necromancer can sacrifice HP at a sealed door to open a forbidden chamber. The reward might be a bone relic, soul skill, or hidden mutation that converts future HP sacrifices into summon strength.
_Novelty_: It turns a dangerous level affix into a class-flavored opportunity, making sacrifice feel like both risk and identity.

**[Combination #33]**: Hunger Debt Skeletons
_Concept_: A Necromancer in a Starved level can create autonomous skeletons by paying HP or max HP, but each summoned minion carries a "hunger debt" that must be repaid through kills, souls, or cleared rooms. If the debt is not repaid, the minion collapses or turns hostile at the exit.
_Novelty_: Summoning becomes a tense loan system rather than free board presence.

**[Combination #34]**: Burning Pyromancer Fire Logic
_Concept_: In a Burning level, the Pyromancer can read and exploit fire rules: standing near flames boosts spells, burning doors can be opened safely, and enemies can be herded through spreading fire. Solving the level means using fire as a routing and combat tool rather than only avoiding it.
_Novelty_: The class turns the room law from hazard into language.

**[Combination #35]**: Overheat Law Puzzle
_Concept_: A Burning level may have an overheat meter that rises with turns, fire spells, or burning tiles. The Pyromancer can intentionally raise, vent, or redirect heat to reveal secret rewards, weaken elites, or unlock the exit before the level becomes too dangerous.
_Novelty_: The player solves the level by manipulating a systemic rule, not by finding a key in the usual sense.

**[Combination #36]**: Mirrored Occultist Curse Toll
_Concept_: In a Mirrored level, the Occultist can pay curses instead of HP, keys, or gold to open mirror doors and reveal hidden routes. The catch is that each curse may be reflected into altered enemy behavior, duplicated hazards, or distorted rewards later in the level.
_Novelty_: Curses become both currency and delayed consequence, fitting the Occultist fantasy of bargaining with unstable powers.

**[Combination #37]**: Reflection Bargain
_Concept_: Mirrored rooms can offer a doubled reward if the player accepts a mirrored penalty, such as duplicating the next elite, reversing a passive, or making healing damage the player for a few turns. The Occultist may have perks that twist these mirrored penalties into advantages.
_Novelty_: The player is not just taking a curse; they are negotiating with symmetry.

**[Combination #38]**: Mirror Debt Doppelganger
_Concept_: A curse toll can create a mirrored copy of the player that appears later in the level, copying some abilities, gear, or recent actions. Defeating it may refund the curse or unlock a hidden reward, while ignoring it could make the level exit more dangerous.
_Novelty_: The cost of a bargain becomes a future tactical encounter shaped by the player's own build.

**[Enemy Ecology #39]**: Mirror Dupe Enemy
_Concept_: A fragile mirrored enemy duplicates itself when damaged from the wrong range, wrong side, or wrong damage type. The player can still defeat it cleanly by learning its pattern or using positioning to prevent the duplicate from becoming dangerous.
_Novelty_: Enemy behavior teaches the player that mirrored levels punish careless symmetry-breaking.

**[Enemy Ecology #40]**: Reversal Knight
_Concept_: A heavily armored enemy reverses one combat rule while present: ranged attacks become weaker, healing creates a shield on the knight, or knockback pulls instead. The player must identify which rule has inverted and adapt.
_Novelty_: It turns a single enemy into a temporary law-bender without changing the entire level.

**[Enemy Ecology #41]**: Glass Prophet Telegraph
_Concept_: A dangerous mirrored caster telegraphs a grand ability by showing the player a reflected future state, such as highlighted tiles or a duplicate spell pattern. The player has one or more turns to break the mirror, move out of the reflected area, or use the projection against enemies.
_Novelty_: Telegraphing becomes diegetic to the mirror theme instead of a generic warning marker.

**[Enemy Ecology #42]**: Reflection Leech
_Concept_: This enemy copies the player's most repeated action over the last few turns, then uses a weaker version against them. If the player varies their actions or manipulates the copy, the leech becomes predictable and exploitable.
_Novelty_: The enemy punishes autopilot play while rewarding deliberate turn variety.

**[Player Tool #43]**: Shatter Tags
_Concept_: Certain attacks, skills, or relics apply a Shatter tag that breaks mirrored copies, disables one reversal rule, or reveals the true version of a duplicated enemy. Shatter effects are especially valuable in Mirrored levels but still useful against illusions elsewhere.
_Novelty_: "Anti-mirror" becomes a recognizable utility keyword that can appear across classes and loot.

**[Player Tool #44]**: Truthstrike Weapon
_Concept_: A weapon affix or skill line deals bonus damage to copied, cursed, or reversed enemies and prevents mirror duplication on killing blows. It may be weaker against normal enemies, making it a strategic tool rather than a universal best weapon.
_Novelty_: The player can deliberately gear for level-law counterplay instead of pure damage.

**[Player Tool #45]**: Cracked Mirror Relic
_Concept_: A relic lets the player break one mirrored rule per level, such as disabling a reversal, revealing a hidden mirror door, or cancelling a reflected penalty. It might recharge when the player clears an odd room or accepts a curse.
_Novelty_: Gives the player agency over oppressive level modifiers without erasing the level's identity.
_Refinement_: This direction is deprioritized because a clickable relic that only counters a random level affinity feels too narrow and artificial.

**[Player Tool #46]**: Shard Counterplay
_Concept_: Breaking mirror effects produces shards that can be spent during the same level for small tactical benefits: reveal a door, damage a duplicate, protect against a reflected attack, or unlock a mirror reward. Shards disappear at the exit.
_Novelty_: Counterplay becomes a temporary resource loop, rewarding players who interact with the level law rather than merely avoiding it.

**[Level Objective #47]**: Mirror Anchor Hunt
_Concept_: A Mirrored level contains key objects such as mirror anchors, cursed glass pylons, reflection idols, or prism seals that sustain the mirror effect. The player can hunt them down and break them to remove or weaken the level's mirror curse.
_Novelty_: Countering a level affinity becomes an exploration and combat objective inside the level, not a UI button.

**[Strategic Choice #48]**: Optional Curse Preservation
_Concept_: The player may choose not to destroy mirror anchors if the mirror effect benefits their build, improves rewards, creates more mirrored enemies to farm, or enables a hidden reward condition. Removing the curse is safer; preserving it can be profitable.
_Novelty_: The level affix becomes a strategic lever rather than a problem with one correct answer.

**[Gear Affix #49]**: Rare Shatter Weapon Enhancement
_Concept_: Shatter can exist as a rare weapon enhancement that deals double or triple damage against mirrored enemies, copies, or curse-created reflections. This makes mirrored levels an opportunity for prepared builds instead of only a threat.
_Novelty_: Anti-affix power comes through general loot and build planning, not a bespoke counter-item.

### Starter Level Affinity Candidates

**[Level Affinity #50]**: Scorched
_Concept_: Fire spreads through parts of the level, changing safe paths, damaging enemies and the player, burning doors open or shut, and creating heat-based secrets. Scorched levels reward players who can route through danger, redirect flames, or exploit enemies that ignite.
_Novelty_: Fire is not only damage over time; it is a shifting routing rule and puzzle element.

**[Level Affinity #51]**: Conductive
_Concept_: Electricity, charged water, metal paths, and unstable lightning objects change movement, targeting, and risk. Conductive levels reward players who can chain lightning, avoid dangerous conductive routes, or turn enemies into circuit pieces.
_Novelty_: The level becomes an electrical circuit where positioning and contact matter.

**[Level Affinity #52]**: Overgrown
_Concept_: Vines, roots, spores, thorns, and living walls alter paths as the level progresses. Nature can block doors, heal enemies, spread poison, entangle movement, or conceal secret routes and rewards.
_Novelty_: The level feels alive and invasive, creating a soft-control affinity rather than a pure hazard.

**[Level Affinity #53]**: Starved
_Concept_: Healing is scarce or weakened, resources feel thin, enemies may feed on low HP, and rewards often ask for sacrifice. Starved levels pressure survival and make every HP payment or risky door feel sharper.
_Novelty_: The affinity attacks comfort and recovery rather than directly attacking the player every turn.

**[Level Affinity #54]**: Timeworn
_Concept_: Parts of the level decay, rewind, age, repeat, or collapse as turns pass. Doors may crumble, rewards may weaken, old enemies may awaken, and some objects may be restored by manipulating timeworn mechanics.
_Novelty_: Turn count becomes visible in the architecture, making delay feel meaningful without using real-time pressure.

**[Level Affinity #55]**: Cursed
_Concept_: The level imposes bargains, corrupted rewards, bad-luck events, cursed doors, and enemies that grow stronger from player greed or repeated choices. Cursed levels tempt players with power while attaching consequences that may follow them beyond the level.
_Novelty_: The affinity is built around temptation and consequence rather than a single environmental element.

**[Level Affinity #306]**: Darkness
_Concept_: Darkness levels greatly reduce information by cutting line of sight, with the draft rule being that player LoS is halved. Unexplored tiles become more threatening, explored-but-unseen areas become less reliable, and enemies or secrets can use darkness as cover.
_Novelty_: Darkness directly attacks the player's planning horizon rather than their HP, making information itself the pressure system.

**Darkness Counterplay:** A Darkness level should include one or more ways to stop, weaken, or locally pierce the darkness, such as lighting braziers, breaking shadow anchors, carrying a torch relic, destroying a gloom idol, spending a consumable flare, or solving a room law. The player should not be forced to clear the level blind if they identify and pursue the counterplay.

**Selected Starter Affinities:** Mirrored, Scorched, Conductive, Overgrown, Starved, Timeworn, Cursed, and Darkness.

**[Secret Design #56]**: Unreliable Secrets
_Concept_: Secrets should not always appear, and even when their clues or patterns appear, the player should not be guaranteed the reward simply because they know the solution from a previous run. Secret availability, route access, required resources, level state, or enemy pressure can vary.
_Novelty_: Discovery remains exciting across runs because knowledge improves odds without removing uncertainty.

**[Secret Design #57]**: Evolving Secret Fail Conditions
_Concept_: Many secrets should have fail conditions that prevent the reward from being achieved, such as taking too many turns, breaking the wrong object, killing a required enemy, leaving an anchor intact, spending the wrong resource, or exiting too early. Some fail conditions can be introduced only after the player successfully solves that secret once, keeping the first discovery generous while future appearances require mastery.
_Novelty_: Secret puzzles evolve from "figure it out" to "execute it under run pressure," preserving replay value.

### Class Candidate Matrix

**Launch Goal:** Start with three classes that strongly differ in positioning, risk appetite, and affinity interaction. Additional classes can be added later if the game gains traction.

**Candidate Classes:**

- **Warrior:** Frontline breacher who turns HP, armor, and adjacency into momentum. Can force doors more cheaply and thrives in Sealed, Starved, and Overgrown levels.
- **Scout:** Mobile path-reader who manipulates information, traps, crits, and secret routes. Strong in Timeworn, Mirrored, and Flooded levels.
- **Pyromancer:** Hazard mage who weaponizes Burning levels, heat, explosions, and area denial. Can solve fire laws and convert dangerous terrain into burst damage.
- **Necromancer:** Sacrifice summoner who pays HP or max HP to create autonomous minions, harvest souls, and exploit Starved or Gravebound-style spaces.
- **Occultist:** Curse gambler who turns bad bargains into power, especially in Cursed and Mirrored levels. Excels at future-consequence builds.
- **Warden:** Defensive controller who shapes space with shields, roots, wards, and terrain protection. Natural fit for Overgrown and Flooded control.
- **Alchemist:** Consumable-adjacent tinkerer without hoarding design, using short-cycle brews, bombs, mutagens, and volatile effects. Good bridge across many affinities.
- **Stormcaller:** Lightning and movement specialist who chains damage through water, metal, and enemy clusters. Especially strong in Flooded levels.
- **Chronomancer:** Turn-economy manipulator who delays, repeats, ages, or rewinds limited events. Strong in Timeworn levels but risky to balance.
- **Duelist:** Precision melee class built around range bands, parries, ripostes, and one-on-one dominance. Clean mobile-friendly tactical identity.
- **Ranger:** Distance and trap class focused on line-of-sight, marks, snares, and safe repositioning. Overlaps Scout unless separated carefully.
- **Druid:** Nature-transformer who can harness Overgrown levels, summon beasts, spread roots, or convert growth into healing and control.
- **Inquisitor:** Anti-curse, anti-mirror, and oath-based class that purges, judges, and gains strength from refusing bargains. Natural counterweight to Cursed/Mirrored content.
- **Berserker:** Low-HP risk class that grows stronger near death and turns Starved levels into opportunity. Needs careful tuning to avoid feeling reckless-only.
- **Runesmith:** Gear and door specialist who inscribes weapons, armor, and level objects with temporary runes. Strong for players who enjoy setup and adaptation.
- **Monk:** Positioning and flow class that gains power from movement patterns, alternating actions, and clean turn sequences. Minimal gear dependency.
- **Beastmaster:** Single hero supported by autonomous animal companions that scout, block, harry, or retrieve. More readable than a full pet-control class.
- **Shadeblade:** Stealth, shadow, and delayed-strike class that manipulates visibility, ambushes, and one-way commitment. Good fit for secrets and risky pathing.

**Selected Starting Class Roster:** Warrior, Pyromancer, Necromancer, Ranger, and Shadeblade.

**Unlock Structure:** Warrior, Pyromancer, and Ranger are available initially. Necromancer and Shadeblade unlock through meta progression, giving the player aspirational class goals after learning the core game.

**Class Coverage:**

- **Warrior:** Melee
- **Pyromancer:** Magic
- **Necromancer:** Summoning
- **Ranger:** Range
- **Shadeblade:** Stealth

### Class Blueprint: Warrior

**[Class Blueprint #58]**: Warrior
_Concept_: The Warrior is the baseline melee class: durable, direct, and built around adjacency, armor, door-breaching, and turning HP pressure into forward momentum. The Warrior should be easy to understand but still tactically rich through positioning and level interactions.
_Novelty_: Warrior is not just "does melee damage"; the class owns physical commitment, forced entry, and controlled risk.

**Fantasy:** A battle-hardened dungeon breaker who survives by standing where others should not, smashing through obstacles, and converting danger into momentum.

**Starting Kit:**

- **Universal Basic Attack:** All classes use the same basic attack action, but the equipped weapon changes its range, pattern, damage profile, and any extra effect attached to the hit.
- **No Level-1 Class Skill:** Classes do not start with active class skills by default. Early class identity should come from passives, starting proficiencies, and equipment choices.
- **Class Passive - Breacher:** When forcing a blocked or locked door, the Warrior has a 50% chance to take no breach damage and a 50% chance to take only half the normal breach damage.
- **Equipment Synergy Passive - Heavy Armor Mastery:** Only half of the normal heavy armor penalty applies. When ending a turn next to an enemy, total armor is increased by 1 for every heavy armor item worn.
- **Starting Gear:** One melee weapon plus armor, with exact item stats rolled inside item-specific ranges.

**Pre-Run Choice Ideas:**

- **Oath of Iron:** Gain armor after forcing doors or entering melee with multiple enemies.
- **Oath of Ruin:** Door breaches and wall slams deal extra damage, but healing is slightly reduced.
- **Oath of Guarding:** Adjacent enemies deal less damage, but ranged enemies are more dangerous.

**Affinity Strengths:**

- **Starved:** Can afford HP pressure better than most and may convert low-HP danger into armor or damage.
- **Overgrown:** Can cut through root doors, thorn walls, or vine barriers.
- **Cursed:** Can choose blunt refusal: resist some bargain penalties, but gains fewer cursed rewards.
- **Timeworn:** Can break decaying objects before they collapse or exploit crumbling terrain for damage.

**Core Build Paths:**

- **Breach Build:** Door-breaking, wall slams, obstacle damage, secret route access.
- **Bulwark Build:** Armor stacking, adjacency defense, retaliation, survival at low HP.
- **Execution Build:** Heavy hits, stuns, armor break, elite dueling.
- **Momentum Build:** Move-then-hit bonuses, knockback chains, room traversal aggression.

**Mobile UX Note:** Warrior should need very few buttons: tap enemy to attack, tap destination to move, drag/target line for Shield Rush. The class teaches spatial basics without opening a complex control panel.

_Revision_: Remove Shield Rush as a level-1 class skill. Warrior should teach the universal action model first: move, basic attack, interact, breach, choose rewards, and exploit equipment.

### Equipment and Proficiency System

**[Core System #59]**: Universal Weapon-Driven Basic Attack
_Concept_: Every class uses the same basic attack command, but the equipped weapon determines how the attack behaves. A sword might attack adjacent enemies reliably, a spear might attack from one tile away, a bow might attack at range, daggers might attack twice or reward flanking, and a staff might convert the basic attack into a magic bolt. Some weapons can also add an extra hit effect, such as bleed, push, burn, pierce, poison, armor break, or Shatter.
_Novelty_: The control scheme stays simple while weapons dramatically change tactical feel.

**[Class Structure #60]**: Passive-First Class Identity
_Concept_: Classes start without active class skills at level 1. Each class begins with one class passive and one equipment-synergy passive, while active abilities can emerge later from level-ups, gear, talents, relics, or run mutations.
_Novelty_: Early onboarding is cleaner, and build identity grows through the run instead of being front-loaded.

**[Item System #61]**: Character-Level Item Requirements
_Concept_: Items can have minimum character level requirements to wield properly, but items themselves do not have upgrade levels like "Sword +3" as their core identity. Instead, each item rolls attributes within ranges and may include special affixes.
_Novelty_: This separates access gating from item scaling, keeping loot readable while preserving excitement from stat rolls and affixes.

**[Item System #62]**: Proficiency-Synergy Passive Model
_Concept_: Each class's equipment-synergy passive defines both what item category they favor and how their proficiency advantage expresses itself. The passive should have two effects: one that reduces or reshapes the cost of using that equipment type, and one that grants a tactical bonus for using it well.
_Novelty_: Weapon and armor proficiency is folded into class identity without adding a separate visible proficiency-growth system too early.

**[Item System #64]**: Run-Based Item Enhancement
_Concept_: Items can be enhanced during a run through events, shrines, smiths, affix forges, sacrifice rooms, or level-affinity interactions. Enhancement improves attributes, adds affixes, changes damage types, or adds conditional effects.
_Novelty_: Loot becomes a relationship across the run, not just a disposable sequence of replacements.

**[Item System #65]**: Weapon Class Conversion Events
_Concept_: Rare events can convert an item into another weapon class while preserving some identity, such as reforging a sword into a spear, corrupting a bow into a shadow crossbow, or turning a staff into a burning blade. This may alter proficiency needs and build direction.
_Novelty_: A single event can create a dramatic build pivot without discarding the player's favorite item.

**[Warrior Passive #66]**: Warrior Heavy Armor Mastery
_Concept_: The Warrior's equipment-synergy passive is Heavy Armor Mastery. Only half of the normal heavy armor penalty applies, and when the Warrior ends a turn next to an enemy, total armor is increased by 1 for every heavy armor item worn.
_Novelty_: The passive has two linked heavy-armor effects: reduced downside and a positioning reward that pushes the Warrior toward brave adjacency.

**[Armor Rule #67]**: Heavy Armor Mobility Penalty
_Concept_: If the player wears at least one heavy armor item, they take a baseline penalty of -1 movement and -5% dodge chance. Heavy Armor Mastery reduces this penalty for the Warrior; because movement values are now small, this likely means the Warrior ignores the movement loss from heavy armor while still taking a reduced dodge penalty.
_Novelty_: Heavy armor's cost is simple and tactical: less mobility and slightly worse evasion, which matters in a turn-based positioning game without adding complex upkeep.

### Class Blueprint: Pyromancer

**[Class Blueprint #68]**: Pyromancer
_Concept_: The Pyromancer is the starter magic class: fragile, explosive, and built around burn effects, heat, hazard manipulation, and turning dangerous level rules into damage. The Pyromancer should feel clever and volatile without needing a level-1 active skill.
_Novelty_: Pyromancer is not just "ranged fire damage"; the class reads fire as a tactical resource and can profit from dangerous Burning spaces.

**Fantasy:** A fire-touched spellcaster who survives by making the level itself unsafe for enemies first.

**Starting Assumption:** Base movement around 3 tiles per turn. Lower armor tolerance than Warrior, better natural synergy with staves, wands, robes, and fire-affix gear.

**Starting Kit:**

- **Universal Basic Attack:** Uses equipped weapon. A staff might create a short-range fire bolt, wand might apply weaker burn at safer range, dagger/sword variants are possible but less natural.
- **No Level-1 Class Skill:** The first-run Pyromancer should teach weapon attacks, burn effects, positioning, and hazard use before adding active spell complexity.
- **Class Passive - Flame Attunement:** The Pyromancer takes 50% damage from fire effects.
- **Equipment Synergy Passive - Staff and Cloth Mastery:** Staves deal 15% increased damage with basic attacks. While wearing only light armor, fire damage is increased by 20%.

**Affinity Strengths:**

- **Burning:** Can exploit fire paths, burning doors, heat meters, and enemies that ignite.
- **Overgrown:** Can burn vines, root walls, spores, and living barriers, but may trigger dangerous spread.
- **Cursed:** Can accept volatile fire bargains for high damage.
- **Flooded:** Weaker if fire is suppressed, unless the build pivots into steam, scalding, or lightning-fire interactions.

**Core Build Paths:**

- **Burn Stack Build:** Apply burn repeatedly, then trigger burst damage or execute burning enemies.
- **Hazard Controller Build:** Spread, redirect, extinguish, or intensify fire zones to shape the level.
- **Explosion Build:** Chain kills, barrels, burning corpses, and volatile affixes.
- **Heat Engine Build:** Build up heat through turns or attacks, then spend it for stronger weapon effects or reward access.

**[Class Mechanic #69]**: Pyromancer Heat Engine
_Concept_: The Pyromancer uses Heat as a small automatic class mechanic. Whenever the Pyromancer deals fire damage or takes fire damage, Heat builds up. When 5 Heat is reached, the next attack deals 30% increased damage, then Heat resets.
_Novelty_: Heat gives the Pyromancer a distinct rhythm without becoming a universal mana system.

_Tuning Note_: Specific percentages and thresholds, such as 5 Heat and 30% increased damage, are draft values for concepting and should be fine-tuned later in development.

**[Class Design Principle #70]**: Nudge-Not-Force Passives
_Concept_: Class passives and equipment passives should encourage a build direction without forcing it. A Pyromancer benefits from staves and fire damage, but should still be able to pivot into a powerful two-handed sword or non-fire build if the run offers rare loot that justifies the shift.
_Novelty_: Class identity becomes a helpful starting current rather than a locked lane, preserving roguelite discovery and exciting off-build pivots.

### Class Blueprint: Ranger

**[Class Blueprint #71]**: Ranger
_Concept_: The Ranger is the starter ranged class: mobile, observant, and built around bows, distance bands, marks, traps, and reading danger before it reaches them. The Ranger should feel tactical through spacing and target choice rather than through stealth, which is reserved more strongly for Shadeblade.
_Novelty_: Ranger is not just "attacks from far away"; the class turns distance, line of sight, and partial information into a survival plan.

**Fantasy:** A sharp-eyed pathfinder who survives by choosing the right target, keeping the right distance, and noticing what others miss.

**Starting Assumption:** Base movement is 4 tiles per turn, making Ranger faster than most ranged or magic classes because movement is part of the class identity. Naturally favors bows, crossbows, thrown weapons, and light armor.

**Starting Kit:**

- **Universal Basic Attack:** Uses equipped weapon. Bows might attack at long range but suffer when adjacent, crossbows might hit harder with shorter range or slower rhythm, and thrown weapons might support closer skirmishing.
- **No Level-1 Class Skill:** The first-run Ranger should teach range, line of sight, target priority, and room reading through weapon behavior and passives.
- **Class Passive - Keen Eye:** The Ranger gets slightly more reliable information from future level or room previews, such as one extra hint about danger, reward type, affinity, enemy family, or hidden route chance.
- **Equipment Synergy Passive - Bow/Crossbow Mastery:** Bow and crossbow basic attacks deal increased damage when no enemy is within 3 tiles of the Ranger. Bows have 1 extra tile of range, and crossbows knock enemies back 1 extra tile.

**[Class Passive #72]**: Ranger Keen Eye
_Concept_: The Ranger receives one additional piece of partial information when choosing future paths or entering a level, such as danger rating, likely enemy family, reward category, affinity clue, or hidden route hint. The information is helpful but never reveals exact room contents with certainty.
_Novelty_: Ranger expresses scouting through the game's pathing system without becoming a separate scouting class.

**[Equipment Passive #73]**: Ranger Bow and Light Armor Mastery
_Concept_: The Ranger's equipment-synergy passive favors bows and crossbows. Bow and crossbow basic attacks deal increased damage when no enemy is within 3 tiles of the Ranger. Bows have 1 extra tile of range, and crossbows knock enemies back 1 extra tile.
_Novelty_: The passive rewards maintaining true distance rather than simply avoiding adjacency, while bows become the reach-focused ranged weapon and crossbows become the control-focused ranged weapon.

**[Class Mechanic #74]**: Ranger Mark Rhythm
_Concept_: Ranger attacks can interact with Marks as a lightweight automatic mechanic. Repeatedly attacking from good range, attacking the same target, or attacking revealed enemies can mark them, making the next hit stronger or adding a utility effect.
_Novelty_: Marks give Ranger a distinct ranged rhythm without requiring active skill buttons at level 1.

**Affinity Strengths:**

- **Timeworn:** Extra path information helps decide when to rush, delay, or avoid decaying routes.
- **Overgrown:** Can pick off spore pods, vine anchors, and dangerous growth objects from range.
- **Flooded:** Benefits from staying out of pull/push hazards, but may need tools against blocked line of sight.
- **Mirrored:** Can identify suspicious paths or duplicated threats earlier, though exact mirror rules stay uncertain.

**Core Build Paths:**

- **Longshot Build:** High damage at ideal distance, line-of-sight rewards, punishes exposed enemies.
- **Marked Prey Build:** Focus targets, stack marks, execute elites or dangerous casters.
- **Trapline Build:** Uses snares, caltrops, tripwires, and terrain control to maintain distance.
- **Pathfinder Build:** Better previews, secret hints, safer routing, and reward-chasing.

**Open Design Question:** What percentage damage bonus should bows and crossbows gain when the Ranger has no enemies within 3 tiles?

### Class Blueprint: Necromancer

**[Class Blueprint #75]**: Necromancer
_Concept_: The Necromancer is an unlockable summoning class: fragile, calculating, and built around death, sacrifice, autonomous minions, and converting losses into future leverage. The Necromancer should feel powerful when a death engine comes together, but risky when HP, corpses, or summon timing are mismanaged.
_Novelty_: Necromancer does not micromanage a party; they create autonomous helpers and death-triggered systems that reshape the level.

**Fantasy:** A grave-touched spellcaster who turns blood, bones, and enemy deaths into temporary allies and forbidden momentum.

**Starting Assumption:** Base movement around 3 tiles per turn. Naturally favors focuses, scepters, daggers, staves, cloth/light armor, and sacrifice or summon affixes.

**Starting Kit:**

- **Universal Basic Attack:** Uses equipped weapon. A focus or scepter might deal weak ranged necrotic damage, daggers support risky close-range sacrifice builds, and staves support safer magic builds.
- **No Level-1 Class Skill:** The Necromancer's early identity should come from death-triggered passives and summon interactions, with active summoning or sacrifice abilities emerging later through gear/talents.
- **Class Passive - Skeleton Harvest:** Killing an enemy has a 20% chance to summon a skeleton that lasts until death. A maximum of 4 skeletons can be alive at the same time; spawning above the limit kills the skeleton with the lowest life, or the oldest skeleton if all are at full health.
- **Equipment Synergy Passive - Tome and Wand Mastery:** Wielding a tome increases skeleton spawn chance by 5%. Wands give 5% life steal when using a basic attack.

**[Class Passive #76]**: Necromancer Skeleton Harvest
_Concept_: Killing an enemy has a 20% chance to summon a skeleton that lasts until death. A maximum of 4 skeletons can be alive at the same time; spawning above the limit kills the skeleton with the lowest life, or the oldest skeleton if all are at full health.
_Novelty_: The class gets summoning identity from level 1 without a summon button, while the skeleton cap keeps board complexity controlled.

**[Equipment Passive #77]**: Necromancer Tome and Wand Mastery
_Concept_: The Necromancer's equipment-synergy passive favors tomes and wands. Wielding a tome increases skeleton spawn chance by 5%, while wands give 5% life steal when using a basic attack.
_Novelty_: Tomes strengthen the summoning path, while wands support a sustain caster path, nudging two necromancer styles without locking out other weapons.

**[Class Mechanic #78]**: Necromancer Soul Ash
_Concept_: Soul Ash is a small automatic mechanic generated when nearby enemies die or summoned minions expire. At a threshold, Soul Ash automatically empowers the next basic attack, raises a temporary minion, shields the Necromancer, or fuels a death-affix reward.
_Novelty_: Soul Ash gives the Necromancer a death rhythm similar in simplicity to Pyromancer Heat, but with a different emotional source.

**Affinity Strengths:**

- **Starved:** Can convert HP pressure, scarcity, and sacrifice into minion or soul value.
- **Cursed:** Benefits from dark bargains and consequences that create more death triggers.
- **Timeworn:** Can exploit old corpses, decayed guardians, or repeated death echoes.
- **Overgrown:** Can clash with living growth or convert dead growth into bone/root minions.

**Core Build Paths:**

- **Bone Guard Build:** Minions block paths, absorb hits, and create safe ranged turns.
- **Soul Engine Build:** Chain enemy deaths into Soul Ash thresholds and empowered attacks.
- **Blood Bargain Build:** Sacrifice HP or max HP for stronger minions and forbidden rewards.
- **Dagger Lich Build:** Off-build melee necromancer using close-range kills to trigger death effects.

**Resolved Design Direction:** Death-triggered skeleton summoning is available from level 1 through Skeleton Harvest.

### Future Class Complexity

**[Progression System #79]**: Future Talent Trees
_Concept_: Classes can gain more complex mechanics later through talent trees or specialization branches. Level-1 class design stays simple with passives and weapon behavior, while later progression can introduce active skills, build-defining mutations, and class-specific subsystems.
_Novelty_: The game can onboard cleanly while still supporting deep class mastery over time.

### Class Blueprint: Shadeblade

**[Class Blueprint #80]**: Shadeblade
_Concept_: The Shadeblade is an unlockable stealth-assassin class: fast, dagger-focused, and built around remaining unseen through positioning, sightlines, stealth effects, items, and later talents. It should feel stealthy in a turn-based way, without relying on positional ambush rules that trigger just because the player stands in a special spot.
_Novelty_: Shadeblade turns being unseen into a tactical state inside the universal turn system.

**Fantasy:** A shadow assassin who survives by choosing when to be seen, striking from advantage, and escaping before the room can answer.

**Starting Assumption:** Base movement is 4 tiles per turn. Naturally favors daggers, short blades, medium armor, light armor, and shadow-affix gear.

**Starting Kit:**

- **Universal Basic Attack:** Uses equipped weapon. Daggers reward unseen attacks through increased proc reliability and synergy with stealth effects.
- **No Level-1 Class Skill:** The first-run Shadeblade should teach detection range, sightlines, being unseen, dagger attacks, and medium armor use before adding active stealth tools.
- **Class Passive - Master of Shadows:** Enemies notice the Shadeblade from 2 fewer tiles away. Unseen damage bonus is increased by 30%.
- **Equipment Synergy Passive - Dagger and Medium Armor Mastery:** Proc chances when wielding daggers are increased by a flat 5% or by 25% of the item's proc rate, whichever is higher. The movement penalty for wearing one or more medium armor items is removed.

**[Armor Rule #81]**: Medium Armor Movement Penalty
_Concept_: If the player wears at least one medium armor item, they take a baseline penalty of -1 movement. Shadeblade's Dagger and Medium Armor Mastery removes this movement penalty.
_Novelty_: Medium armor offers protection with a lighter mobility cost than heavy armor, and Shadeblade can use it without losing assassin tempo.

**[Class Passive #82]**: Shadeblade Master of Shadows
_Concept_: Enemies notice the Shadeblade from 2 fewer tiles away. Unseen damage bonus is increased by 30%, making stealth and sightline control more rewarding without granting stealth automatically from generic positioning.
_Novelty_: The class improves the unseen state itself rather than creating artificial ambush triggers.

**[Equipment Passive #83]**: Shadeblade Dagger and Medium Armor Mastery
_Concept_: The Shadeblade's equipment-synergy passive favors daggers and medium armor. Proc chances when wielding daggers are increased by a flat 5% or by 25% of the item's proc rate, whichever is higher, and the movement penalty for wearing one or more medium armor items is removed.
_Novelty_: Daggers become the proc-heavy assassin weapon, while medium armor makes the Shadeblade less fragile without slowing them down.

**[Class Mechanic #84]**: Shadeblade Unseen State
_Concept_: Shadeblade's key state is being unseen, achieved through positioning outside enemy detection range, line-of-sight breaks, stealth abilities, stealth items, and later talent options. A future talent may grant an active Stealth ability that turns the Shadeblade invisible for a few tile movements on a short cooldown.
_Novelty_: Stealth is grounded in visibility and tools rather than arbitrary positional ambush rules.

**Affinity Strengths:**

- **Mirrored:** Can exploit shadows, false copies, and mirror anchors, but risks being exposed by reflection effects.
- **Cursed:** Can accept shadow bargains for burst damage, secret access, or unseen damage enhancement.
- **Timeworn:** Can race decay timers or use collapsing paths to break sightlines.
- **Overgrown:** Can use cover, blocked sightlines, and hidden paths to become or remain unseen.

**Core Build Paths:**

- **Unseen Burst Build:** Maximize first-hit damage, crits, poison, or bleed from unseen state.
- **Evasion Build:** Dodge, light armor, threat-zone avoidance, and turn-efficient repositioning.
- **Secret Killer Build:** Gains advantages from secret routes, hidden doors, odd rooms, and risky shortcuts.
- **Shadow Curse Build:** Trades safety for stronger unseen damage, mirrored tricks, or cursed mobility.

**Future Talent Note:** Shadeblade may later gain an active Stealth talent that makes them invisible for a few tile movements with a short cooldown, but level-1 class identity should stay passive-first.

### Equipment and Loot System

**[Equipment System #85]**: Equipment Slot Model
_Concept_: The player equips a compact set of items: weapon, off-hand or focus slot where relevant, armor pieces, and a small number of accessory/relic slots. The exact slot count should stay mobile-readable, likely fewer than a traditional RPG inventory paper doll.
_Novelty_: Equipment can support RPG depth without overwhelming the phone UI.

**[Equipment System #86]**: Weapon Attack Profiles
_Concept_: The universal basic attack changes based on weapon class. Daggers are low-damage melee weapons that become exceptional from Unseen attacks, swords are reliable melee, spears attack from 2 tiles away, axes trade some sword damage for bleed chance, maces trade some sword damage for disorient chance, bows gain range, crossbows push, staves cast bolts, and wands support precise magical basic attacks.
_Novelty_: One input produces many tactical identities through gear instead of class buttons.

**[Equipment System #86A]**: Support Items
_Concept_: Tomes and shields are not basic-attack weapons by default. Tomes provide stats or modifiers, such as increased damage from a source or improved summon chance. Shields provide armor and a percentage chance to block half incoming physical damage.
_Novelty_: Off-hand/support items shape builds without forcing every item to become an attack profile.

**[Armor System #87]**: Armor Category Rules
_Concept_: Armor categories define tradeoffs. Light armor has no movement penalty and may support dodge or elemental builds; medium armor gives more protection but normally applies -1 movement if any medium armor is worn; heavy armor gives strong protection but applies -1 movement and -5% dodge if any heavy armor is worn.
_Novelty_: Armor choice affects tactical movement immediately, which is more meaningful than passive defense alone.

**[Item System #88]**: Item Roll Bands
_Concept_: Items do not have item levels, but each item type rolls attributes within defined ranges. A sword might roll damage, crit, and armor break chance, while a robe might roll dodge, fire damage, and resistance.
_Novelty_: Drops stay exciting through variation without relying on escalating "+1, +2, +3" item tiers.

**[Item System #89]**: Affix Count by Rarity
_Concept_: Rarity controls how many special properties an item can have and how strange those properties may be. Common items are readable stat sticks, uncommon items have one meaningful affix, rare items can define a build direction, and legendary/unique items can bend rules.
_Novelty_: Rarity is about build implications, not only bigger numbers.

**[Item System #90]**: Affinity-Touched Loot
_Concept_: Level affinities can influence loot affixes. Burning levels can drop scorched weapons, Flooded levels can drop conductive gear, Overgrown levels can drop thorned or regenerative items, Starved levels can drop sacrifice/sustain items, Timeworn levels can drop decay/rewind gear, Cursed levels can drop powerful items with consequences, and Mirrored levels can drop Shatter or reflection items.
_Novelty_: The dungeon's current rules shape the loot ecosystem, making pathing decisions influence build evolution.

**[Run Progression #91]**: Enhancement Rooms
_Concept_: Special rooms can enhance a carried item by improving a roll, adding an affix, changing damage type, adding a proc, or binding the item to an affinity. These rooms create mid-run attachment to gear.
_Novelty_: The player can invest in a promising item instead of replacing everything constantly.

**[Run Progression #92]**: Reforge Events
_Concept_: Rare events can transform an item into another weapon or armor class while preserving part of its identity. A dagger might become a shadow spear, a staff might become a burning blade, or a bow might become a cursed crossbow.
_Novelty_: Reforging creates dramatic build pivots and lets a strange run become memorable.

**[Combat System #93]**: Proc Language
_Concept_: Items can have proc effects with clear trigger wording, such as "on basic attack," "on kill," "when unseen," "when taking fire damage," "when ending near an enemy," or "when entering a new room." Proc chances should be visible and modified by passives like Shadeblade's dagger mastery.
_Novelty_: Proc builds become legible rather than hidden math.

**[Loot Flow #94]**: Loot Choice Triads
_Concept_: Important loot moments should often present 3 choices rather than dumping many items at once. Choices can include a safe upgrade, a synergy piece, and a risky off-build temptation.
_Novelty_: Loot becomes a decision point instead of inventory cleanup.

**[Item System #95]**: No-Hoarding Consumables
_Concept_: Consumables, if used, should avoid the classic hoarding problem. They can be temporary level tools, auto-use thresholds, short-lived charges, or room-bound resources rather than permanent potions the player saves forever.
_Novelty_: The game preserves active decision-making and avoids dead inventory weight.

**[Loot Philosophy #96]**: Build Pivot Drops
_Concept_: Rare and powerful non-synergistic drops should be allowed to redirect a run. Passives nudge class identity, but a great two-handed sword on a Pyromancer or a cursed bow on a Shadeblade can become a valid story.
_Novelty_: Roguelite joy comes partly from the run arguing back against the player's plan.

### Equipment and Loot System Extensions

**[Equipment System #97]**: Dual Weapon Slots
_Concept_: Characters have two weapon slots. Two-handed weapons exist and occupy the relevant weapon capacity, while not all classes can dual-wield. The off-hand can hold non-main-weapon items such as shields, tomes, throwing weapons, or other class-relevant tools.
_Novelty_: Weapon setup becomes a build choice without requiring every class to play like a dual-wielder.

**[Weapon Rule #98]**: Staff Range Melee Split
_Concept_: Staff attacks are ranged projectiles by default. If the target is adjacent, the staff attack becomes melee and deals 50% of its normal damage.
_Novelty_: Staves remain flexible but clearly punish letting enemies close the gap.

**[Weapon Rule #99]**: Wand Instant Line
_Concept_: Wands deal less damage than staves, but their damage is instant and is not blocked by enemies or objects.
_Novelty_: Wands become the reliable precision caster weapon, trading raw damage for clean targeting.

**[Weapon Rule #100]**: Adjacent Ranged Penalty
_Concept_: Ranged weapons deal 30% less damage when targeting an enemy adjacent to the player.
_Novelty_: Range matters as a tactical condition, not just a weapon label.

**[Equipment System #101]**: Armor and Jewelry Slots
_Concept_: Armor comes in light, medium, and heavy classes. The character has slots for body armor, helmet, boots, gloves, and pants, plus two ring slots and one amulet slot.
_Novelty_: The equipment layout supports build variety while staying familiar and readable.

**[Loot Flow #102]**: Mixed Loot Sources
_Concept_: Special rewards often use 3-choice moments, but normal enemies can also have a low chance to drop random loot, special enemies have a higher chance, and loot can appear on tiles in levels, reward rooms, and hidden areas.
_Novelty_: Loot can surprise the player during exploration without replacing meaningful reward choices.

**[Inventory System #103]**: Six Item Backpack
_Concept_: The player has a small backpack, initially storing around 6 total items. Nothing stacks in the inventory. Passives, talents, items, or other progression may increase inventory size.
_Novelty_: Inventory tension prevents hoarding while keeping individual item choices meaningful.

**[Consumable System #104]**: Clever Consumables
_Concept_: Consumables are semi-rare and encouraged to be used, but can also enable clever rule-breaking. A fire breath potion might let a non-fire build burn vines blocking a secret area, opening a path they would otherwise miss.
_Novelty_: Consumables become tactical keys and opportunity tools, not just emergency potions.

**[Pickup System #105]**: Instant Pickups
_Concept_: Enemies and level objects can drop instant pickups such as health globes, XP globes, temporary buffs, or short-lived effects. For example, an XP globe might grant double XP for the next three kills.
_Novelty_: Pickups create small tactical incentives inside combat and exploration without adding backpack clutter.

**[Passive Loot #106]**: Passive Pickups
_Concept_: The game includes passive loot pickups that permanently or semi-permanently alter the current run. These can be simple stat boosts, weapon bonuses, build synergies, or major rule-bending effects.
_Novelty_: Passive pickups become the main source of roguelite build weirdness and discovery.

**[Passive Loot #107]**: Rule-Bending Passives
_Concept_: Some passives can bend core rules dramatically: basic attacks are always 1 tile range but cleave up to three enemies; health globes damage the player but poison heals for double; the player cannot move but can teleport once per turn up to 10 tiles; or a bloodseeker curse causes bleeding each turn while granting strong lifesteal and close-combat damage.
_Novelty_: Passives can create entirely new run identities and make apparent downsides become hidden synergy opportunities.

**[Passive Loot #108]**: Downside-First Synergy Passives
_Concept_: Some passive pickups may appear to only have downsides until the player discovers or assembles a synergy that makes them powerful. Rewards may offer a specific passive or a choice of three passives.
_Novelty_: The game rewards experimentation, memory, and creative build reading rather than only obvious upgrades.

### Expanded Affinity-Touched Loot Taxonomy

**[Loot Affinity #109]**: Scorched
_Concept_: Formerly "Burning." Focuses on fire, heat, ignite, ash, explosions, burning terrain, and overheat rewards.
_Novelty_: Scorched better describes both fire damage and aftermath-themed loot.

**[Loot Affinity #110]**: Conductive
_Concept_: Formerly "Flooded." Focuses on lightning, electricity, charged water, metal conduction, stun chains, and electric movement hazards.

**[Loot Affinity #111]**: Frozen
_Concept_: Focuses on ice, brittle armor, slowed movement, sliding, chill stacks, shatter damage, and frozen barriers.
_Novelty_: Frozen gives the game a control-heavy cold family distinct from water/electricity.

**[Loot Affinity #112]**: Radiant
_Concept_: Focuses on blessed, holy, anti-undead, cleansing, healing light, protection, and damage against ghosts or corrupted enemies.
_Novelty_: Radiant offers a clean counterweight to Cursed, Hallowed, and Necromancer-adjacent content.

**[Loot Affinity #113]**: Legendary
_Concept_: Focuses on rare, epic upgrades to normal weapon or armor modifiers: stronger stat ranges, upgraded core affixes, and powerful versions of standard item identities.
_Novelty_: Legendary is not an element; it is a prestige modifier family that makes normal gear feel mythic.

**[Loot Affinity #114]**: Thorned
_Concept_: Split from Overgrown. Focuses on thorns, retaliation, bleed-on-contact, punishing adjacency, and damage when enemies move through growth.
_Novelty_: Thorned supports defensive and trap-like builds.

**[Loot Affinity #115]**: Regen
_Concept_: Split from Overgrown. Focuses on healing over time, recovery triggers, living armor, regrowth, and sustain that rewards survival positioning.
_Novelty_: Regen gives nature loot a sustain identity separate from poison or roots.

**[Loot Affinity #116]**: Root
_Concept_: Split from Overgrown. Focuses on immobilize, entangle, path blocking, living walls, terrain creation, and enemy movement denial.
_Novelty_: Root becomes the nature control family.

**[Loot Affinity #117]**: Fungal
_Concept_: Focuses on poison, spores, corrosion, armor reduction, infection spread, delayed damage, and dangerous growth that can benefit or punish the player.
_Novelty_: Fungal creates a darker biological family distinct from wholesome regen or root control.

**[Loot Affinity #118]**: Arcane
_Concept_: Focuses on magic amplification, spell-like basic attacks, mana-adjacent effects if later introduced, random magic mutation, and interactions with staves, wands, tomes, and enchanted gear.
_Novelty_: Arcane gives non-elemental magic its own loot space.

**[Loot Affinity #119]**: Void/Abyssal
_Concept_: Focuses on gravity, crushing, pulling, pushing, throwing, forced movement, collapse, and space-warping effects.
_Novelty_: Void/Abyssal makes movement manipulation feel cosmic and dangerous.

**[Loot Affinity #120]**: Gale/Zephyr
_Concept_: Focuses on wind, movement speed, dodge, repositioning, knockback, range manipulation, and airborne or light-footed effects.
_Novelty_: Gale/Zephyr creates a mobility family that can support Ranger, Shadeblade, and off-build speed runs.

**[Loot Affinity #121]**: Maddening
_Concept_: Focuses on loss of control in exchange for power, confusion, enemies attacking their own kind, unreliable targeting, panic, frenzy, and sanity-bending rewards.
_Novelty_: Maddening creates dangerous power that can be brilliant or disastrous.

**[Loot Affinity #122]**: Hallowed
_Concept_: Focuses on summoning, ghosts, undead, grave rites, spirits, temporary allies, and death-adjacent power without being purely evil or cursed.
_Novelty_: Hallowed gives ghostly and summoning builds a sacred/deathly alternative to Necromancer darkness.

**Current Affinity Note:** Older references to Burning and Flooded should be read as Scorched and Conductive going forward. Overgrown can remain a level theme, while loot affixes split into Thorned, Regen, Root, and Fungal families.

**[Affinity System #123]**: Level and Item Affinity Separation
_Concept_: Level/room affinities and weapon/item affinities can overlap but do not have to be identical taxonomies. Some affinities may make sense primarily for levels, such as Flooded, while others may make sense primarily for items, such as Legendary, and some can exist in both spaces.
_Novelty_: This avoids forcing every environmental theme into loot and every loot family into level generation.

**[Reward System #124]**: Class-Tailored Reward Pools
_Concept_: A run can include class-specific passives, modifiers, and reward variants based on the class currently being played, but the level should not block progress or deny interaction because the player chose a different class. For example, Necromancer-specific summon modifiers can appear only for Necromancer runs, and Hallowed item affixes may include summon-related variants for Necromancer while rolling from non-summon pools for other classes.
_Novelty_: Class identity can enrich rewards without creating "wrong class" doors or dead options.

### Core Combat Rules

**[Combat Rule #125]**: Player-Action Turn Clock
_Concept_: Every committed player action advances enemy behavior: moving, attacking, using an item, interacting with a door/object, or waiting. The player can think indefinitely before acting, but once they act, the dungeon answers.
_Novelty_: The game creates pressure through turn commitment rather than real-world time.

**[Combat Rule #126]**: Tile Movement Budget
_Concept_: Each class has a base movement budget per turn, such as Warrior 4, Pyromancer 3, Ranger 4, Necromancer 3, and Shadeblade 4. Armor, items, passives, roots, ice, wind, and other effects can modify this budget.
_Novelty_: Movement becomes a visible tactical resource that classes and gear can meaningfully shape.

**[Combat Rule #127]**: Move-Attack Flexibility
_Concept_: A turn can allow movement, one basic attack, and possibly one interaction depending on final tuning. The simplest early model should avoid action-point bookkeeping while still forcing the player to choose when movement exposes or protects them.
_Novelty_: Keeps mobile turns fast without losing tactical consequence.

**[Combat Rule #128]**: Universal Basic Attack
_Concept_: Every class uses the same basic attack command, but weapon class determines range, targeting, damage profile, and hit effects. Weapon choice is therefore a combat-rule choice, not only a stat choice.
_Novelty_: The player learns one input and many tactical outcomes.

**[Combat Rule #129]**: Range Pressure
_Concept_: Ranged weapons deal 30% less damage when targeting adjacent enemies. Staffs become melee at adjacent range and deal 50% damage, while wands deal lower damage but ignore blocking enemies and objects.
_Novelty_: Closing distance against ranged characters matters immediately.

**[Combat Rule #130]**: Armor Mitigation and Dodge
_Concept_: Armor reduces incoming damage or adds effective survivability, while dodge gives a chance to avoid attacks. Heavy armor reduces movement and dodge, medium armor reduces movement, and light armor preserves mobility.
_Novelty_: Defensive choices are tied to spatial play rather than pure numbers.

**[Combat Rule #131]**: Enemy Detection and Threat
_Concept_: Enemies notice, pursue, or target the player based on detection range, line of sight, noise, affinity rules, and class modifiers. Shadeblade reduces enemy notice range by 2 tiles.
_Novelty_: Detection supports stealth without adding real-time sneaking.

**[Combat Rule #132]**: Enemy Pattern Literacy
_Concept_: Enemies generally do not reveal exact next actions, but each enemy family has learnable behavior. Major dangerous abilities can be telegraphed one or more turns in advance.
_Novelty_: The player improves through knowledge without turning every turn into perfect-information chess.

**[Combat Rule #133]**: Object and Hazard Interaction
_Concept_: Combat includes doors, anchors, braziers, vines, conductive pools, mirror objects, cursed shrines, loot tiles, and other interactables. These can be attacked, triggered, breached, burned, frozen, moved, or exploited depending on gear and affinity.
_Novelty_: The level is part of combat, not a backdrop.

**[Combat Rule #134]**: Summon Autonomy
_Concept_: Summons such as Necromancer skeletons act autonomously using simple predictable rules: attack nearby enemies, block paths, follow loosely, or defend the player. They are not directly micromanaged.
_Novelty_: Summoning adds board texture without creating a party tactics UI.

**[Combat Rule #135]**: Pickup Timing
_Concept_: Pickups such as health globes, XP globes, and temporary buffs appear on tiles and matter tactically because reaching them costs movement and advances turns.
_Novelty_: Even a health pickup can become a positioning decision under pressure.

**[Combat Rule #136]**: Death Spiral Resistance
_Concept_: The combat model should avoid unavoidable death spirals. Low HP can be tense and build-relevant, but players should often see risky ways out: pickups, doors, consumables, enemy luring, hazard use, or secret exits.
_Novelty_: Tension stays exciting instead of feeling predetermined.

**[Combat Rule #137]**: Elemental and Affinity Interactions
_Concept_: Damage types, tile states, enemy states, and affinities should interact in surprising but learnable ways. Killing a Frozen enemy with fire damage can trigger a Thermal Shock explosion; electricity damage hitting water can spread through connected water tiles; arrows fired through fire can ignite and deal fire damage or burn; Necromancer talents might summon skeletons attuned to the damage affinity that killed the enemy.
_Novelty_: Combat becomes systemic, letting clever players create outcomes that are not hardcoded as single-use abilities.

### Level Generation

**[Level Generation #137]**: Forward-Only Level Structure
_Concept_: Each level is a connected sequence or small graph of rooms, doors, corridors, hazards, rewards, and exits. The player cannot backtrack to previous rooms once they commit forward.
_Novelty_: Routing becomes a chain of meaningful commitments rather than exhaustive map clearing.

**[Level Generation #138]**: Level Contains Rooms
_Concept_: A level may contain multiple rooms with different local purposes: combat, reward, shrine, locked chamber, hazard puzzle, secret room, elite room, shop-like event, or exit room. Rooms are small enough for mobile tactical readability.
_Novelty_: The game keeps dungeon-crawl flavor while preserving compact encounter design.

**[Level Generation #139]**: Clear Exit With Optional Deviations
_Concept_: Every level should communicate a clear exit path or objective, but optional side rooms, locked doors, breach routes, hidden exits, and risky reward branches can complicate the decision.
_Novelty_: The player always knows how to leave, but not always whether leaving is the best choice.

**[Level Generation #140]**: Partial Preview Routing
_Concept_: Before entering the next level or major room branch, the player sees partial information such as level type, likely affinity, danger rating, reward hint, enemy family clue, or unusual anomaly. The preview is never fully certain.
_Novelty_: Pathing feels strategic without becoming solved.

**[Level Generation #141]**: Affinity Layering
_Concept_: A level can have zero, one, or occasionally multiple level affinities. Affinities define environmental rules, hazards, objects, enemy variants, secret logic, and reward tendencies, but they do not have to match item-affinity names exactly.
_Novelty_: Level identity can vary deeply without requiring a unique tileset every time.

**[Level Generation #142]**: Affinity Objects
_Concept_: Level affinities are often expressed through objects the player can interact with, attack, preserve, or exploit. Examples include mirror anchors, fire braziers, flooded conduits, root hearts, cursed idols, timeglass statues, or fungal pods.
_Novelty_: Affinity counterplay happens inside the level instead of through abstract modifiers.

**[Level Generation #143]**: Secret Chance and Fail States
_Concept_: Secrets do not always appear, and when they do, they often include fail conditions such as too many turns, destroying the wrong object, taking a specific reward first, killing a required enemy, missing a clue, or leaving the affinity intact/removed at the wrong moment.
_Novelty_: Knowing a secret pattern improves the player's odds without guaranteeing the reward.

**[Level Generation #144]**: First-Discovery Grace
_Concept_: The first time a player discovers a secret type, its fail condition can be forgiving or absent. After the player has solved it once, future appearances can introduce stricter execution requirements.
_Novelty_: Secrets teach before they test.

**[Level Generation #145]**: Breachable and Locked Doors
_Concept_: Doors can be unlocked, locked, sealed, hidden, breachable, cursed, or affinity-bound. Some can be opened with keys, HP sacrifice, consumables, level-object manipulation, or specific damage types. Classes may make some approaches easier through passives, but doors should not require a specific class.
_Novelty_: Door decisions become part of combat, build identity, and resource economy.

**[Level Generation #146]**: Dynamic Level State
_Concept_: Levels can change as turns pass or rooms are cleared: fire spreads, water conducts, roots grow, curses deepen, time decays structures, anchors maintain mirror effects, or fungal infection expands.
_Novelty_: The level feels alive without requiring real-time pressure.

**[Level Generation #147]**: Reward Room Variety
_Concept_: Reward spaces can include direct loot, passive choices, enhancement rooms, reforge events, consumables, pickups, hidden caches, affinity shrines, class-tailored reward events, and dangerous bargain rooms.
_Novelty_: Rewards become contextual moments, not just chests.

**[Level Generation #148]**: Level Size Bands
_Concept_: Levels can vary in size from compact 2-3 room slices to larger 6-10 room expeditions, with run length targets in mind. Longer levels should contain more exit pressure, optional branches, and meaningful stopping points.
_Novelty_: Session length can vary while the core structure remains familiar.

**[Level Generation #149]**: No-Backtracking Loot Tension
_Concept_: Because the player cannot backtrack, visible but unreachable rewards, forked doors, and side rooms create real tension. Taking one branch may permanently sacrifice another.
_Novelty_: The map itself creates roguelite regret and replayability.

**[Level Generation #150]**: Level End State
_Concept_: Exiting a level resolves temporary level effects, clears short-lived pickups/resources, and presents rewards, previews, or path choices for the next step. Some curses, item changes, passive effects, and meta rewards persist.
_Novelty_: Level transitions become clean mobile-friendly checkpoints.

### Enemy Families

**[Enemy Design #151]**: Enemy Family Philosophy
_Concept_: Enemies should belong to families with learnable behaviors rather than only isolated stat blocks. A player should gradually understand how beasts, cultists, undead, constructs, fungi, spirits, elementals, and assassins usually behave, while affinities and elites modify those expectations.
_Novelty_: Mastery comes from pattern literacy without full next-action previews.

**[Enemy Family #152]**: Beasts
_Concept_: Beasts are movement-pressure enemies: wolves leap, boars charge, spiders web, bats swarm, and larger beasts punish poor positioning. They tend to be readable and physical.
_Novelty_: Beasts teach spacing, threat zones, and movement economy early.

**[Enemy Family #153]**: Cultists
_Concept_: Cultists use rituals, curses, ranged attacks, summons, and affinity objects. They may protect idols, channel delayed spells, or empower other enemies.
_Novelty_: Cultists make the player decide whether to attack enemies, objects, or ritual sources first.

**[Enemy Family #154]**: Undead
_Concept_: Undead resist simple attrition through resurrection, bones, ghosts, curses, and death triggers. Skeletons may reassemble, zombies may keep advancing, and ghosts may ignore some terrain.
_Novelty_: Undead create cleanup problems and synergize strongly with Hallowed, Radiant, Cursed, and Necromancer-related systems.

**[Enemy Family #155]**: Constructs
_Concept_: Constructs are armored, predictable, and rule-bound. They may guard doors, anchor level mechanics, patrol fixed routes, or activate when the player breaches seals.
_Novelty_: Constructs reward planning and armor-breaking rather than raw speed.

**[Enemy Family #156]**: Fungal Growths
_Concept_: Fungal enemies spread spores, poison, corrosion, armor reduction, infection, and terrain hazards. They may be weak individually but dangerous if allowed to propagate.
_Novelty_: Fungal enemies create expanding problems that tempt fire, cleanse, or fast objective play.

**[Enemy Family #157]**: Spirits
_Concept_: Spirits phase, haunt, drain, confuse, or attach to objects and enemies. They may be hard to pin down but vulnerable to Radiant, Hallowed, or specific reveal mechanics.
_Novelty_: Spirits support eerie tactical disruption without always being direct damage dealers.

**[Enemy Family #158]**: Elementals
_Concept_: Elementals embody affinities such as Scorched, Frozen, Conductive, Void, Gale, or Arcane. They interact heavily with tile states and elemental combo rules.
_Novelty_: Elementals teach systemic combat interactions through enemy behavior.

**[Enemy Family #159]**: Bandits and Hunters
_Concept_: Humanoid tactical enemies use weapons, traps, flanking, ranged pressure, and retreat behavior. Archers punish open spaces, rogues seek unseen attacks, and shieldbearers protect allies.
_Novelty_: They mirror player-like tactics and make positioning feel contested.

**[Enemy Role #160]**: Grunts
_Concept_: Grunts are straightforward enemies that establish the basic threat of a family. They should be readable, quick to resolve, and useful for teaching weapon and movement rules.
_Novelty_: Simple enemies make the weird systems legible by contrast.

**[Enemy Role #161]**: Controllers
_Concept_: Controllers push, pull, root, slow, silence, blind, reposition, or manipulate terrain. Their danger comes from changing the board state rather than dealing huge damage.
_Novelty_: Controllers make movement and tile state matter.

**[Enemy Role #162]**: Casters
_Concept_: Casters use delayed spells, curses, summons, area effects, or affinity interactions. Major caster abilities should often be telegraphed so the player can interrupt, move, or exploit them.
_Novelty_: Casters create priority decisions and dramatic "stop this now" moments.

**[Enemy Role #163]**: Brutes
_Concept_: Brutes are high-health, high-impact enemies that punish standing still, blocking paths, or fighting in cramped rooms. They may be slow but force routing decisions.
_Novelty_: Brutes turn space into pressure.

**[Enemy Role #164]**: Skirmishers
_Concept_: Skirmishers are mobile enemies that dart in and out, flank, flee, or force the player to spend movement inefficiently. They challenge Rangers and casters by closing gaps.
_Novelty_: Skirmishers prevent combat from becoming static.

**[Enemy Role #165]**: Summoners
_Concept_: Summoners create temporary minions, revive corpses, call beasts, split fungi, or animate objects. They are usually fragile enough that reaching them matters.
_Novelty_: Summoners create urgent pathing puzzles.

**[Enemy Role #166]**: Anchors
_Concept_: Anchor enemies or objects sustain a level-affinity effect, such as mirror distortion, fungal spread, conductive charge, or time decay. Destroying or preserving them changes the level state.
_Novelty_: Anchors connect enemy design directly to level objectives.

**[Enemy Role #167]**: Elites
_Concept_: Elites are upgraded enemies with one or more modifiers, stronger stats, and higher loot chances. They may carry affinity variants or special object interactions.
_Novelty_: Elites are tempting risk/reward targets and natural loot spikes.

**[Enemy Role #168]**: Bosses and Finale Guardians
_Concept_: Bosses should test a run's build coherence, not only raw stats. They may include phases, summons, arena objects, affinity rules, and telegraphed high-danger abilities.
_Novelty_: The final challenge asks whether the player's build engine actually came together.

**[Enemy Modifier #169]**: Affinity Variants
_Concept_: Enemy families can be modified by level affinities. A Scorched wolf might leave fire trails, a Conductive construct might chain lightning through water, a Timeworn cultist might repeat its last spell, and a Mirrored beast might split when hit incorrectly.
_Novelty_: Reusing families with affinity variants creates breadth without requiring endless bespoke enemies.

**[Enemy Loot Rule #170]**: Enemy Loot Chance
_Concept_: Normal enemies have a low chance to drop random loot or pickups, while special enemies, elites, and bosses have higher chances. Drops can be influenced by enemy family, level affinity, and current run reward pools.
_Novelty_: Combat can produce surprise rewards while preserving special reward rooms as major loot moments.

**[Enemy Modifier #171]**: Elite Enemy Spawns
_Concept_: Any enemy type can rarely spawn as an elite version of itself, gaining increased stats, stronger behavior, and a higher chance to drop rewards or higher-rarity loot. Elite enemies create moment-to-moment risk/reward spikes inside normal levels.
_Novelty_: A familiar enemy can suddenly become a meaningful tactical and loot opportunity.

**[Passive Loot #172]**: Elite-Rich Run Modifier
_Concept_: A rule-bending passive could set enemy elite chance to a fixed high value, such as 25%. This makes the run much more dangerous, but an already powerful player may choose it to accelerate loot quality and build scaling.
_Novelty_: The player can deliberately make the dungeon richer and deadlier when their build feels ready.

**[Enemy Modifier #173]**: Enemy Affinity Spawns
_Concept_: Enemies can spawn with an affinity modifier. A Scorched enemy might take reduced fire damage, increased frozen/water damage, and deal fire damage with its attacks. Affinities change both weaknesses and threat profile.
_Novelty_: Affinity enemies encourage adaptive damage types and tactical reading.

**[Enemy Modifier #174]**: Elite Affinity Mini-Bosses
_Concept_: Rarely, an enemy can spawn as both elite and affinity-touched, effectively becoming a random mini-boss. These enemies should be dangerous, visibly distinct, and more rewarding.
_Novelty_: The game can create emergent mini-boss moments without hand-authoring every encounter.

**[MVP Scope #175]**: Initial Enemy Families
_Concept_: The MVP enemy-family set should start with Cultists, Undead, and Demons. Other families can be saved for expansion once the core combat, level, and loot systems work.
_Novelty_: Starting with three families keeps production scope manageable while covering ritual/caster, death/summon, and aggressive supernatural identities.

**[Level Generation #176]**: Enemy Family Pool Per Level
_Concept_: Each level selects enemy families from a pool. Most levels use one primary family, while larger or special levels can use two families for variety and cross-family tactics.
_Novelty_: Enemy composition becomes part of the level's identity and preview language.

**[Level Affinity #177]**: Chaotic
_Concept_: A Chaotic level can draw enemies randomly from all available families, creating unpredictable encounters and strange combinations. Chaotic levels should feel less controlled but potentially more rewarding.
_Novelty_: Chaotic is the "anything can happen" level modifier that breaks normal family expectations.

**[Level Generation #178]**: Special Compact Levels
_Concept_: Some special levels can be extremely compact and contain little or no combat, such as a Wheel of Fortune level with only the wheel, a shrine, a bargain room, or a single strange event.
_Novelty_: Not every level needs to be a combat dungeon slice; special levels can provide pacing breaks and high-impact decisions.

**[Level Generation #179]**: Level Size Categories
_Concept_: Normal levels can use visible size categories: Small, Medium, Large, and Huge. Small levels aim for roughly 1-3 minutes, Medium levels 3-6 minutes, Large levels 5-10 minutes, and Huge levels 5-20 minutes depending on player power, thoroughness, and speed.
_Novelty_: Size becomes part of path strategy and session planning.

**[Level Generation #180]**: Rare Huge Levels
_Concept_: Huge levels should be uncommon and typically appear as selectable path choices only around 1-2 times per run on average. They should feel like major expedition opportunities rather than normal pacing.
_Novelty_: Huge levels become memorable commitments instead of exhausting defaults.

**[Level Preview #181]**: Always Visible Size Clue
_Concept_: When choosing the next path, the level's size category should always be visible. Even if affinity, enemy family, secrets, or rewards are hidden or partial, the player should know whether they are choosing a Small, Medium, Large, Huge, or Special level.
_Novelty_: The player can manage real-life session length while still facing in-game uncertainty.

**[Run Map #182]**: Seeded Run Map
_Concept_: The overall map/path structure is seeded when the run begins, including branching structure and level size positions. The player usually sees only the next set of level choices. The seed should be visible after death and in the menu during a run, and players should be able to replay an almost exact copy of a run from that seed.
_Novelty_: The run has coherent structure while preserving discovery, and visible seeds support replayability, testing, troubleshooting, and bug reports.

**[Run Map #183]**: Scout Horizon
_Concept_: By default, players can scout only the next choice of levels ahead. Passives, talents, items, or class effects can reveal more information, such as additional branches, affinity hints, enemy family clues, secret chance, reward type, or deeper map nodes.
_Novelty_: Scouting becomes a valuable build and reward axis.

**[Run Map #184]**: Lazy Visible Modifier Generation
_Concept_: The map structure is seeded at run start, but affinities and modifiers are generated when the player can see or scout a level. Once generated and shown, those details should be locked for fairness.
_Novelty_: Future-level generation can respond to the player's evolving build without changing already-observed information.

**[Run Map #185]**: Build-Influenced Future Affinities
_Concept_: Hidden thresholds or build signals can influence future visible level generation. For example, carrying several Scorched items could increase the chance that future scoutable level choices include Scorched affinities or related modifiers.
_Novelty_: The dungeon can subtly answer the player's build without feeling fully deterministic.

**[Level Preview #186]**: Modifier Chance Manipulation
_Concept_: Passives, talents, items, and events can modify the odds that future levels roll certain affinities, modifiers, sizes, reward types, or enemy compositions, as long as this affects only not-yet-visible levels.
_Novelty_: Map shaping becomes a strategic reward type.

**[Level Design Guardrail #187]**: Lock Revealed Information
_Concept_: Any level details already revealed to the player, such as size, affinity, enemy family, or reward hints, should not silently change afterward. Build-responsive generation should affect future unseen choices only.
_Novelty_: The system can stay dynamic without betraying player trust.

### Run Map Generation Recipe

**[Map Recipe #188]**: Node-Based Run Map
_Concept_: A run uses a branching node map. Each node represents a level, special event, reward space, elite encounter, shop-like event, or boss/finale step. The full node graph is seeded when the run begins, but most node details remain hidden until they enter the player's scout horizon.
_Novelty_: The map gives structure and long-term pathing decisions without revealing the whole run's tactical contents up front.

**[Map Recipe #189]**: Run Length Bands
_Concept_: A typical full run can aim for roughly 8-12 chosen nodes before the finale, depending on level size distribution and player choices. Full runs usually land around 10-60 minutes, short failed runs can end much faster, and rare long or thoughtful runs can exceed 60 minutes.
_Novelty_: Run pacing supports both mobile sessions and deep "one more level" commitment.

**[Map Recipe #190]**: Map Skeleton Generation
_Concept_: At run start, generate the map skeleton: number of columns/depth steps, branching connections, guaranteed boss/finale location, approximate size distribution, and special node slots. This skeleton does not yet need final enemy families, affinities, or modifiers for hidden nodes.
_Novelty_: The run has a deterministic backbone while tactical details can adapt later.

**[Map Recipe #191]**: Size Distribution
_Concept_: Medium nodes should be most common. Small nodes provide quick pacing relief, Large nodes offer bigger commitments, Huge nodes appear rarely as major optional expeditions, and Special nodes provide high-impact nonstandard choices.
_Novelty_: Size distribution creates rhythm, not just difficulty scaling.

**Suggested Starting Distribution:**

- **Special:** 10-15%
- **Small:** 20-25%
- **Medium:** 40-50%
- **Large:** 15-20%
- **Huge:** rare, around 1-2 selectable opportunities per run

**[Map Recipe #192]**: Visible Size, Hidden Details
_Concept_: Size is always visible on every currently selectable node. Other details, such as affinity, enemy family, reward hint, secret chance, elite chance, and special modifiers, can be hidden, partially hinted, or revealed by scouting.
_Novelty_: The player can manage time commitment while still facing strategic uncertainty.

**[Map Recipe #193]**: Scout Horizon Resolution
_Concept_: By default, when the player reaches a map decision point, the next set of selectable nodes enters scout horizon. At that moment, those nodes roll and lock visible/scoutable details such as affinity, enemy family, modifiers, and reward hints.
_Novelty_: The run can react to the current build while preserving trust once information is shown.

**[Map Recipe #194]**: Locked Once Scouted
_Concept_: Once a node has been scouted in any way, all generated details for that node are locked. Later passives, thresholds, or probability changes cannot mutate that already-scouted node.
_Novelty_: Player planning remains fair and reliable.

**[Map Recipe #195]**: Build-Responsive Future Rolls
_Concept_: When an unseen node enters scout horizon, its affinity/modifier rolls may be influenced by current build signals, such as carrying many Scorched items, having several summon passives, stacking curses, or investing in scouting talents.
_Novelty_: The dungeon feels responsive to the run's emerging identity without rewriting known choices.

**[Map Recipe #196]**: Node Detail Roll Order
_Concept_: When a node becomes scoutable, roll details in a consistent order: level category, enemy family pool, level affinity if any, special modifiers, elite/affinity enemy odds, reward hint, secret chance, and visible preview clues.
_Novelty_: A consistent generation order makes the system easier to tune and debug later.

**[Map Recipe #197]**: Enemy Family Selection
_Concept_: Most combat nodes select one primary enemy family from the available pool. Larger levels can occasionally select two families. Chaotic levels can pull from all available families.
_Novelty_: Enemy family composition becomes a previewable strategic variable.

**[Map Recipe #198]**: Affinity Selection
_Concept_: A level may have no affinity, one affinity, or rarely multiple modifiers. Affinities should shape objects, hazards, enemy variants, secrets, and reward tendencies, but they should not require matching item-affinity names.
_Novelty_: Affinities become level personality rather than a mandatory elemental checklist.

**[Map Recipe #199]**: Path Choice Texture
_Concept_: Each map decision should usually offer 2-3 meaningful next-node choices. Good choices contrast size, danger, reward, family hints, affinity clues, special events, or path position rather than only offering three versions of the same combat node.
_Novelty_: The map becomes a strategic layer, not a decorative loading screen.

**[Map Recipe #200]**: Guaranteed Pacing Beats
_Concept_: A run should include a few guaranteed or high-likelihood pacing beats, such as an early reward/passive choice, at least one enhancement or reforge opportunity, occasional special events, and a clear finale path.
_Novelty_: Randomness remains exciting without accidentally starving the player of build-shaping opportunities.

**[Map Recipe #201]**: Huge Level Placement
_Concept_: Huge levels should usually be optional branches with strong preview clues and meaningful rewards. They should rarely block the critical path unless the run is intentionally entering a major finale-adjacent expedition.
_Novelty_: Huge levels feel like chosen commitments rather than pacing traps.

**[Map Recipe #202]**: Map Preview Clue Types
_Concept_: Preview clues can include size, rough type, danger rating, enemy family hint, affinity hint, reward category, elite presence, secret possibility, special event marker, or instability marker. Scout builds can reveal more clue types.
_Novelty_: Scouting upgrades can increase decision quality without revealing everything.

**[Map Recipe #203]**: Finale Route
_Concept_: The map should always lead toward a finale/boss sequence, but optional branches can improve, distort, or complicate the player's build before reaching it. Some secret exits or risky routes may alter finale conditions.
_Novelty_: The final boss is a destination the player prepares for through route choices.

### Procedural Level Construction Details

**[Level Construction #204]**: Tile Budget by Size
_Concept_: Each level size should have a rough traversable tile budget, not just a time estimate. Suggested starting ranges: Special 4-40 tiles, Small 35-80, Medium 80-160, Large 160-280, Huge 280-500. These are traversable/interactable tiles, excluding fully blocked wall space.
_Novelty_: Tile budgets give generation a concrete pacing target while leaving room shape flexible.

**[Level Construction #205]**: Room Count by Size
_Concept_: Suggested room counts: Special 1 room, Small 1-3 rooms, Medium 3-6 rooms, Large 5-9 rooms, Huge 8-14 rooms. Rooms can be combat chambers, corridors, bridges, puzzle spaces, reward pockets, exit chambers, or secret rooms.
_Novelty_: The level can feel larger without requiring one giant unreadable grid.

**[Level Construction #206]**: Room Shape Library
_Concept_: Levels should be assembled from a library of room shapes and room roles: square chambers, narrow halls, bridge crossings, pit arenas, pillar rooms, loop rooms, fork rooms, shrine rooms, dead-end reward pockets, locked rooms, and exit rooms.
_Novelty_: A shape library creates variety while reducing impossible or ugly procedural layouts.

**[Level Construction #207]**: Anchor-Based Generation
_Concept_: Each generated level starts by placing required anchors: entrance, main exit, critical path rooms, optional branch points, reward anchors, enemy encounter anchors, affinity object anchors, secret anchors if rolled, and special-object anchors. Generation then fills supporting geometry around those anchors.
_Novelty_: Anchors ensure important gameplay promises exist before decorative randomness begins.

**[Level Construction #208]**: Critical Path First
_Concept_: Generate the critical path from entrance to exit first, then attach optional branches, locked side rooms, secret paths, rewards, and hazards. The critical path should always be valid without requiring a specific class, rare item, or secret.
_Novelty_: The player can always progress, while optional risk remains meaningful.

**[Level Construction #209]**: Branch Budget
_Concept_: Each size band has a branch budget: Small 0-1 branches, Medium 1-2, Large 2-4, Huge 3-6. Branches can include rewards, elites, secrets, shortcuts, shrines, affinity-object routes, or dangerous detours.
_Novelty_: Branching scales with level size and prevents small levels from becoming cluttered.

**[Level Construction #210]**: Enemy Budget
_Concept_: Each level rolls an enemy budget based on size, family, affinity, and difficulty depth. Suggested baseline: Small 2-5 enemies, Medium 5-10, Large 9-16, Huge 15-28, with elites counting as multiple budget points.
_Novelty_: Enemy count can be tuned independently from room count so levels avoid feeling empty or overcrowded.

**[Level Construction #211]**: Encounter Anchors
_Concept_: Enemies should spawn around encounter anchors rather than randomly anywhere. Anchors can define patrols, ambush points, guarded objects, ritual clusters, choke points, ranged nests, or brute blockers.
_Novelty_: Enemy placement feels intentional and readable instead of noisy.

**[Level Construction #212]**: Spawn Safety Rules
_Concept_: Initial enemy placement should avoid unfair starts: no unavoidable immediate lethal damage, no enemy fully surrounding the player at entrance, no blocking the only exit without counterplay, and no ranged enemy firing from unreachable positions.
_Novelty_: Procedural danger stays fair.

**[Level Construction #213]**: Object Budget
_Concept_: Levels roll an object budget for walls, pits, bridges, doors, cover, loot tiles, hazard sources, affinity anchors, shrines, traps, and interactables. The budget scales by size and affinity.
_Novelty_: Objects become a controlled part of encounter design instead of decorative scatter.

**[Level Construction #214]**: Traversal Validation
_Concept_: After generation, run path validation from entrance to exit and to all intended reachable key anchors. The generator should reject levels where the critical path is blocked, required doors have no valid open method, or rewards/secrets are placed outside their intended access logic.
_Novelty_: Bad layouts are caught automatically before the player sees them.

**[Level Construction #215]**: Tactical Space Validation
_Concept_: Validate that rooms have enough playable space for the selected enemy family and player movement budget. Avoid tiny rooms with too many brutes, ranged nests with no cover, bridges too narrow for summons, or pits that invalidate melee pathing.
_Novelty_: Layout quality is checked against combat needs, not only path connectivity.

**[Level Construction #216]**: Secret Injection Pipeline
_Concept_: If a secret is rolled, choose a secret template that declares its required anchors, clue type, access condition, fail condition, and reward room type. The generator reserves space for those anchors before finalizing the map.
_Novelty_: Secrets are generated as structured mini-systems, not hidden tiles sprinkled randomly.

**[Level Construction #217]**: Secret Template Example
_Concept_: A hidden reward room template might require: clue object near branch, locked/hidden door anchor, optional fail condition, secret reward anchor, and alternate exit or one-way return. If any anchor cannot be placed validly, the secret is rerolled or skipped.
_Novelty_: Secret rooms can be reliable enough to function while still feeling mysterious to the player.

**[Level Construction #218]**: One-Way Commitment Gates
_Concept_: No-backtracking can be enforced through one-way doors, collapsing bridges, sealing thresholds, moving walls, trap gates, or map-level commitment. These gates should be visually clear before crossing whenever possible.
_Novelty_: The player feels commitment, not cheap surprise.

**[Level Construction #219]**: Affinity Object Placement
_Concept_: Affinity objects should be placed where their decisions matter: mirror anchors on optional routes or protected by enemies, Scorched braziers near vines/oil/doors, Conductive nodes near water/metal paths, Timeworn statues near decaying routes, and Cursed idols near tempting rewards.
_Novelty_: Affinity objects become tactical and routing choices rather than background props.

**[Level Construction #220]**: Reward Placement Rules
_Concept_: Rewards should be placed behind decisions: after combat, behind risk doors, at branch ends, after secret execution, beside danger, or as a choice between alternatives. Avoid placing meaningful rewards where optimal play always collects them for free.
_Novelty_: Loot acquisition becomes part of the level's decision structure.

**[Level Construction #221]**: Generation Retry and Repair
_Concept_: The generator should use validation retries first, then limited repair passes if needed. Repairs can add a bridge, widen a choke, move an enemy anchor, add a door key, remove an impossible hazard, or downgrade a secret if placement fails.
_Novelty_: Bad layouts can be corrected without over-flattening procedural variety.

**[Level Construction #222]**: Mobile Readability Constraint
_Concept_: Individual visible room chunks should fit comfortably on a mobile screen or scroll/pan in a controlled way. Avoid huge open arenas with too many simultaneous objects; prefer connected readable spaces.
_Novelty_: The generation respects the target device, not just abstract dungeon design.

**[Summon UX #223]**: Player-Summon Swap Movement
_Concept_: If the player tries to move onto a tile occupied by one of their summoned creatures, the player swaps places with that summon instead of being blocked. This should apply to friendly autonomous summons such as Necromancer skeletons unless a specific effect says otherwise.
_Novelty_: Summons add tactical texture without creating frustrating pathing clutter.

**[Reward UX #224]**: Passive Reward Decision Modal
_Concept_: When the player steps onto a passive-modifying reward, the game opens a modal showing the passive icon, name, flavor text, and concrete mechanical effects. The player can inspect inventory, character stats, and current build before choosing.
_Novelty_: Major build-altering decisions get a moment of clarity and drama instead of being accidentally picked up.

**[Reward UX #225]**: Consume or Destroy Passive Rewards
_Concept_: Passive rewards present an irreversible choice: Consume it or Destroy it. Consuming adds the passive to the run. Destroying rejects it and may sometimes grant a smaller generic or thematic benefit.
_Novelty_: Even an off-build passive can still become a meaningful decision rather than dead loot.

**[Reward Example #226]**: Destroyed Passive Conversion
_Concept_: If a summon-focused passive such as "Phylactery of Undeath" appears in a run that has moved away from summoning, destroying it could apply a lesser world effect, such as reducing max HP for future undead enemies.
_Novelty_: Destroying a passive can create a consolation prize that still respects the passive's theme.

**[Save System #227]**: Between-Level Save Checkpoints
_Concept_: The game can use save checkpoints between levels so players can safely stop after completing a level and making map/reward decisions.
_Novelty_: Fits mobile play while preserving run continuity.

**[Save System #228]**: Fixed Loaded Level Seed
_Concept_: Once a player loads into a level, that level's seed and generated layout/rewards should be fixed. Force-quitting mid-level should not reroll the level, its layout, its major reward opportunities, or its already-generated passive choices.
_Novelty_: Prevents save-scumming for a different level or different major reward outcome.

**[Save System #229]**: Runtime Variance Boundary
_Concept_: Some minor events may still be generated during play, such as random item/globe drops from enemies, exact enemy spawn point variance, or elite spawn chance procs. If a player save-scums those small runtime events, that is acceptable.
_Novelty_: The game protects major run integrity without needing to deterministically freeze every tiny combat event.

**[Save System #230]**: Major-Minor Determinism Split
_Concept_: Major authored/generated facts should be deterministic once visible or loaded: level layout, node identity, secret existence, main reward anchors, passive reward options, and affinity rules. Minor stochastic combat rewards can remain probabilistic at runtime.
_Novelty_: This draws a practical boundary between anti-save-scum design and acceptable roguelite randomness.

### Meta Progression

**[Meta Progression #231]**: Meta Progression Philosophy
_Concept_: Meta progression should primarily unlock variety, knowledge, classes, starting options, talent depth, and new reward pools rather than simply making the player numerically stronger every run. Failed runs should feel productive, but the game should remain skillful and tactically meaningful.
_Novelty_: Progression creates freshness and mastery without turning the roguelite into pure grind.

**[Meta Progression #232]**: Run Reward Currency
_Concept_: Runs can award a meta currency based on progress, level clears, bosses defeated, secrets found, elite kills, first-time discoveries, and challenge modifiers. Any run longer than a few minutes should grant some progress.
_Novelty_: Even failed attempts create forward motion without requiring the player to win.

**[Meta Progression #233]**: Class Unlocks
_Concept_: Warrior, Pyromancer, and Ranger are available initially. Necromancer and Shadeblade unlock through meta progression, achievements, or milestone discoveries.
_Novelty_: Unlocking advanced classes gives players aspirational goals after learning the core systems.

**[Meta Progression #234]**: Class Talent Trees
_Concept_: Each class can have a talent tree that unlocks specialization options, alternate passive variants, active abilities, class-specific passive pools, and run-start choices. Talent trees should add complexity gradually.
_Novelty_: Classes can deepen over time without making level-1 onboarding heavy.

**[Meta Progression #235]**: Talent Loadout Limits
_Concept_: Even if players unlock many talents, they should not activate everything at once. A class might have a limited number of pre-run talent slots, branch choices, or mutually exclusive specializations.
_Novelty_: Meta progression creates interesting preparation choices rather than permanent power bloat.

**[Meta Progression #236]**: Starting Choice Expansion
_Concept_: Meta progression can unlock new starting weapons, armor, consumables, passive pools, map-preview options, or class oaths. These should offer different openings rather than always being strict upgrades.
_Novelty_: Players gain more ways to begin a run without trivializing early levels.

**[Meta Progression #237]**: Reward Pool Unlocks
_Concept_: Discovering secrets, defeating bosses, clearing affinities, or unlocking classes can add new passives, item affixes, level events, enemy variants, and reward templates to future runs.
_Novelty_: The game world expands as the player proves they can handle more complexity.

**[Meta Progression #238]**: Knowledge Codex
_Concept_: A codex can record encountered enemies, affinities, passives, secrets, elemental interactions, elite modifiers, and class mechanics. It should reveal information through discovery rather than spoiling everything immediately.
_Novelty_: Player knowledge becomes a persistent reward without directly increasing stats.

**[Meta Progression #239]**: Secret Mastery Tracking
_Concept_: Once the player discovers and completes a secret type, the game can track mastery and later introduce stricter fail conditions or advanced variants for that secret.
_Novelty_: Secrets can evolve over time while preserving the joy of first discovery.

**[Meta Progression #240]**: Difficulty and Challenge Modifiers
_Concept_: Later progression can unlock optional challenge modifiers that increase rewards: higher elite chance, more affinity enemies, fewer previews, stronger bosses, reduced healing, larger levels, or chaotic enemy pools.
_Novelty_: Skilled players can choose more danger for richer loot and faster meta progress.

**[Meta Progression #241]**: Build Archive
_Concept_: After each run, the game can summarize the player's final build, notable passives, strongest item, elite kills, secret discoveries, and cause of death or victory. This can become a lightweight run history.
_Novelty_: Runs become memorable stories and learning artifacts.

**[Meta Progression #242]**: New Affinity Unlocks
_Concept_: Not every item or level affinity needs to be available from the first run. More complex families such as Maddening, Void/Abyssal, Arcane, Hallowed, or Legendary can unlock after the player understands the basics.
_Novelty_: Complexity ramps over time and keeps later runs surprising.

**[Meta Progression #243]**: Merchant and Workshop Unlocks
_Concept_: Meta progression can unlock optional between-run systems such as a workshop, blacksmith, archive, trainer, or strange merchant. These can expand item enhancement, starting options, class talents, or codex knowledge.
_Novelty_: A light hub gives progression a home without requiring a heavy story layer.

**[Meta Progression #244]**: Avoid Mandatory Stat Grind
_Concept_: Permanent raw stat boosts, if present at all, should be limited, capped, or side-grade oriented. The player's main improvement should come from knowledge, unlocks, buildcraft, and tactical skill.
_Novelty_: The game stays fair and replayable instead of making early losses feel like required farming.

**[Meta Progression #245]**: First-Win and Post-Win Expansion
_Concept_: The first full victory can unlock a broader game layer: additional affinities, harder map modifiers, more bosses, advanced class talents, or alternative finales.
_Novelty_: The game can start approachable and then unfold into deeper roguelite complexity.

**[Run Economy #246]**: Gold
_Concept_: Gold is an in-run currency that can drop occasionally from enemies, appear on tiles, and be found by breaking objects. Gold can be spent during the current run on gambling events, shop levels, blacksmith reforges, and other temporary opportunities.
_Novelty_: Gold creates short-term economy decisions without becoming permanent progression.

**[Meta Currency #247]**: Diamonds
_Concept_: Diamonds, or a similar meta currency, are earned during runs through first-time clears, specific events, boss kills, hard achievements, challenge completions, and other milestone moments. Diamonds persist between runs and fund permanent unlocks or upgrades.
_Novelty_: Meta currency rewards both progress and mastery.

**[Progression System #248]**: In-Run Class Talent Trees
_Concept_: Each class can have an in-run talent tree that develops during a single run, letting the player specialize their current build with temporary class upgrades, active abilities, passive modifiers, or build branches.
_Novelty_: A class can evolve differently each run without requiring permanent commitment.

**[Progression System #249]**: Class Mastery
_Concept_: Each class has a Class Mastery progression tree where diamonds can be spent for permanent future upgrades, unlocks, alternate starts, talent options, reward pool additions, and class-specific progression.
_Novelty_: Long-term class mastery gives each class its own progression journey.

**[Progression System #250]**: General Meta Talent Tree
_Concept_: In addition to class-specific meta trees, the game can include a general meta talent tree for account-wide or game-wide upgrades, such as broader reward pools, map scouting options, shop unlocks, codex features, or challenge modifiers.
_Novelty_: Players can invest in global systems without needing to main only one class.

**[Meta Progression #251]**: Content Unlock Progression
_Concept_: A major part of meta progression is unlocking new enemies, loot, passives, item affixes, secrets, bosses, level events, affinities, and reward templates. Unlocks should expand possibility space as the player gains experience.
_Novelty_: The game grows wider and stranger over time rather than only increasing numbers.

**[UI System #252]**: Codex Pane
_Concept_: The Codex pane records discovered enemies, level affinities, item affinities, passives, secrets, elemental interactions, elite modifiers, bosses, and class mechanics. Entries should reveal through encounter and discovery.
_Novelty_: Knowledge becomes a persistent form of progression.

**[UI System #253]**: Achievements and Challenges Pane
_Concept_: The Achievements/Challenges pane tracks one-time achievements, run challenges, class challenges, secret objectives, boss feats, and difficult optional goals that can award diamonds, unlocks, cosmetics, or new content.
_Novelty_: Challenges create directed goals for players who want structure beyond surviving the run.

**[UI System #254]**: Class Mastery and Progression Pane
_Concept_: The Class Mastery/Progression pane shows each class's unlock status, meta talent tree, mastery milestones, class-specific challenges, discovered class passives, and future reward pool unlocks.
_Novelty_: Class progression becomes visible and motivating instead of hidden behind vague currency spending.

**Terminology Note:** Use "Class Mastery" instead of "class meta talent tree" going forward.

### What If Scenarios

**Technique Goal:** Deliberately mutate core assumptions to discover distinctive hooks, surprising systems, and future expansion ideas. These are not commitments yet; they are creative pressure tests.

**[What If #255]**: Dungeon-Fed Broken Builds
_Concept_: The dungeon can sometimes offer dangerous synergies after the player leans into a build direction, but these offers should not guarantee success. Players can still screw up their build, and interactions should not always be obvious.
_Novelty_: The game supports discovery, mistakes, and surprising power spikes rather than perfectly transparent optimization.

**[What If #256]**: Wrong-Clear Hidden Achievements
_Concept_: Some levels can have hidden "wrong way" clear conditions, such as preserving a dangerous object, refusing healing, letting a hazard spread, or clearing under a strange restriction. These rewards should primarily grant meta currency rather than guaranteed in-run power.
_Novelty_: Wrong-clears become mastery achievements without making every level's secret mandatory for optimal runs.

**[What If #257]**: Remembered Passive Decisions
_Concept_: The game can remember how the player treats passive rewards, such as consuming, destroying, rejecting, or favoring certain types. Future levels, rewards, or hidden triggers can subtly respond to these decisions.
_Novelty_: The run gains memory without requiring explicit story dialogue.

**[What If #258]**: Adaptive Endgame Resistances
_Concept_: Harder endgame bosses can have a higher chance to roll resistance against the player's main damage output or strongest build theme. This should test backup plans without fully hard-countering the run.
_Novelty_: Strong builds still feel strong, but finale fights ask whether the build has depth.

**[What If #259]**: Chained Cross-Level Secrets
_Concept_: Some secrets can span multiple levels: a clue, item state, destroyed object, preserved curse, or earlier decision unlocks an opportunity in a later level. These should be rare and discoverable through pattern recognition.
_Novelty_: The run map becomes a longer puzzle, not just isolated levels.

**[What If #260]**: Destroy-Streak Hidden Passives
_Concept_: Hidden passives can unlock when the player repeatedly makes unusual choices, such as destroying 5 passive pickups in a row without accepting one. The reward could be a purifier/salvager-style passive, rule change, or synergy.
_Novelty_: Refusal becomes a build path.

**[What If #261]**: Death-Bargain Alternate Path
_Concept_: Under specific conditions, death may not end the run but instead trigger a rare Starved, Cursed, Hallowed, or Abyssal bargain that sends the player onto a different level path with a different endgame boss. This is separate from normal free resurrection items or talents.
_Novelty_: A failed run can transform into a stranger run instead of simply ending.

**[What If #262]**: Resurrection Streak Hidden Passive
_Concept_: If the player achieves something rare like being resurrected 3 times in the same run, a hidden passive or theme-related bonus can unlock. This could support death-cheating, Hallowed, Cursed, or undead-adjacent builds.
_Novelty_: Extreme survival stories can become mechanical unlocks.

**[What If #263]**: Reward Corruption Ritual
_Concept_: A cultist or similar enemy can start a ritual at the beginning of a level that will corrupt, alter, steal, or transform a reward unless killed within a turn countdown. The countdown may be visible without fully explaining what it affects.
_Novelty_: Rewards become vulnerable objectives inside combat.

**[What If #264]**: Corrupted Reward Ambiguity
_Concept_: A corrupted item or passive is not always worse. Some builds may benefit from the corruption, while others lose a clean reward they wanted. This makes reward-tampering enemies dangerous but not one-dimensionally bad.
_Novelty_: The player may sometimes choose to let the ritual finish.

## MVP Scope

**MVP Goal:** Build a playable vertical slice that proves the core loop: choose a seeded map node, enter a procedurally generated forward-only level, fight turn-based tactical enemies, collect loot/passives, exit, gain progression, and repeat.

### MVP Must-Haves

**[MVP #265]**: Playable Run Loop
_Concept_: The MVP should support starting a run, selecting map nodes, entering levels, clearing/exiting levels, receiving rewards, continuing to the next node, dying, and seeing a run summary.
_Why MVP_: This proves the game is a roguelite loop rather than isolated combat tests.

**[MVP #266]**: Seeded Run Map
_Concept_: The MVP should generate a seeded branching map with visible level sizes and a simple scout horizon. The seed should be visible in-run and after death, and replaying a seed should reproduce the major run structure.
_Why MVP_: Seeds are useful for testing, debugging, player replay, and bug reports.

**[MVP #266a]**: Seed Replay No Meta Progress
_Concept_: Runs started from a manually entered seed should not grant meta currency, achievements, unlocks, challenge credit, or other permanent progression. They are for replay, practice, testing, sharing, and bug reproduction.
_Why MVP_: Prevents players from farming known favorable seeds while preserving the usefulness of seed replay.

**[MVP #267]**: Basic Level Generator
_Concept_: Generate Small, Medium, and Large levels using entrance, exit, critical path, room shapes, enemy anchors, object anchors, and validation. Huge and complex special levels can be deferred.
_Why MVP_: Procedural levels are central, but scope should begin with manageable size bands.

**[MVP #268]**: No-Backtracking Progression
_Concept_: Levels should enforce forward-only movement or room commitment in a simple way, such as one-way doors, sealed transitions, or map-level commitment.
_Why MVP_: This is a defining design pillar and affects every routing decision.

**[MVP #269]**: Core Turn-Based Combat
_Concept_: Implement player movement budgets, enemy turns after player actions, HP, armor, dodge, basic attacks, detection basics, and simple enemy behavior.
_Why MVP_: Combat feel must be proven before adding too many affinities or passives.

**[MVP #270]**: Universal Basic Attack With Weapon Types
_Concept_: Implement a small starting attack-weapon set such as sword, dagger, spear, axe, mace, bow, crossbow, staff, and wand, plus support/off-hand items such as tome and shield. Weapon class changes basic attack range/behavior according to the established rules, while support items modify stats, defenses, or build direction.
_Why MVP_: Weapon-shaped basic attacks are one of the game's biggest differentiators.

**[MVP #271]**: Three Initial Classes
_Concept_: MVP should include Warrior, Pyromancer, and Ranger as the first playable classes. Necromancer and Shadeblade can be present as locked placeholders or deferred until after core systems are stable.
_Why MVP_: Three classes prove melee, magic, and ranged play without exploding scope.

**[MVP #272]**: Starter Enemy Families
_Concept_: MVP can start with Cultists, Undead, and Demons, but only a few enemy roles per family are needed initially: grunt, caster/controller, brute, elite.
_Why MVP_: This proves family identity and tactical variety while staying feasible.

**[MVP #273]**: Elite and Affinity Enemy Variants
_Concept_: Implement elite enemies and one simple affinity variant system. Elite + affinity combinations can appear rarely as mini-boss-like encounters.
_Why MVP_: This proves the risk/reward enemy modifier layer early.

**[MVP #274]**: Limited Affinity Set
_Concept_: MVP should include only a small affinity set, likely Scorched, Conductive/Flooded, Cursed, and maybe Frozen or Hallowed. Other affinities can be content expansions.
_Why MVP_: Affinity interactions are rich but can balloon quickly.

**[MVP #275]**: Equipment, Inventory, and Loot
_Concept_: Implement equipment slots, two weapon slots, armor slots, rings/amulet, small 6-item backpack, non-stackable inventory, random loot drops, and 3-choice special rewards.
_Why MVP_: Loot decisions are central to build formation.

**[MVP #276]**: Passive Reward Modal
_Concept_: Passive pickups open a modal with icon, name, flavor, exact effects, inventory/stat access, and irreversible Consume/Destroy choices.
_Why MVP_: Passive rewards are one of the game's strongest signature systems.

**[MVP #277]**: Small Passive Pool
_Concept_: Implement a compact set of simple, synergistic, and rule-bending passives. Include at least a few downside-first passives and destroy benefits.
_Why MVP_: The game needs enough passives to show build mutation without requiring hundreds of effects.

**[MVP #278]**: Gold and Basic Shops/Reforge
_Concept_: Implement gold drops from enemies/tiles/objects and at least one gold sink, such as shop levels, gambling events, or blacksmith reforge.
_Why MVP_: Gold gives in-run economy purpose.

**[MVP #279]**: Diamonds and Basic Meta Progression
_Concept_: Implement diamonds as meta currency from boss kills, first-time clears, achievements, or run milestones. Start with a simple general progression screen and placeholder Class Mastery.
_Why MVP_: Failed runs need persistent value, but deep progression can come later.

**[MVP #280]**: Save/Seed Determinism Rules
_Concept_: Implement between-level saves, fixed loaded level seeds, visible run seeds, and deterministic major generation once nodes/levels are visible or loaded.
_Why MVP_: This supports mobile play and debugging from the start.

**[MVP #281]**: Base Run Node Count
_Concept_: A base full run should target around 11-14 selected nodes including the final node, with most successful runs landing around 60 minutes depending on level sizes, player speed, and decision time. A good first target is 10-12 pre-finale nodes plus one finale/boss node.
_Why MVP_: This gives enough room for build formation without making every successful run sprawl.

**[MVP #282]**: Pacing-Responsive Node Budget
_Concept_: The run can adjust effective path length based on selected level sizes. If the player clears several Small or Special nodes, the run may add or expose an extra node opportunity so the build still has time to mature. If the player takes multiple Large or Huge nodes, the route can stay shorter.
_Why MVP_: Node count should serve expected run time and build pacing, not be a rigid number.

**[MVP #283]**: Latent Map Nodes for Pacing
_Concept_: If the run map is seeded at start, pacing-responsive extra nodes should come from pre-seeded latent/optional nodes rather than generating brand-new structure from nowhere. These nodes can become visible only when pacing conditions call for them.
_Why MVP_: Preserves seed reproducibility while allowing run length to flex.

### Explicitly Defer From MVP

- Huge levels as full expeditions
- Deep secret chains across multiple levels
- Full Class Mastery trees
- Necromancer and Shadeblade fully playable, unless core systems progress quickly
- Large affinity taxonomy
- Complex boss adaptation
- Death-bargain alternate routes
- Extensive codex and achievements
- Large passive/item pools
- Advanced elemental interaction matrix

### MVP Success Criteria

- A player can complete or die in a seeded run.
- The run produces at least one interesting build decision.
- At least one level feels meaningfully different from another.
- Weapon choice changes how the basic attack feels.
- Passive Consume/Destroy choices feel consequential.
- The level generator produces valid layouts reliably.
- The run seed can reproduce major structure for debugging.

## First Playable Prototype Plan

**Prototype Goal:** Build the smallest playable version that proves the core loop is fun before investing in deep content.

### Phase 1: Tactical Room Prototype

**Goal:** Prove turn-based movement and basic attack feel.

**Includes:**

- Grid/tile movement
- Player movement budget
- Enemy response after player action
- HP, damage, armor, dodge placeholder
- 2-3 weapon types: sword, bow, staff
- 2-3 simple enemies
- Win/lose condition for one test room

**Success Test:** Moving, attacking, and enemy turns feel understandable and tactically interesting.

### Phase 2: Weapon-Shaped Combat Prototype

**Goal:** Prove universal basic attack + weapon identity.

**Includes:**

- Sword, dagger, spear, axe, mace, bow, crossbow, staff, wand
- Tome and shield as support/off-hand modifiers
- Adjacent ranged penalties
- Staff melee penalty
- Wand ignores blockers
- Basic hit effects/procs
- Simple UI for weapon comparison

**Success Test:** Swapping weapons changes decisions, not just numbers.

### Phase 2 Weapon Identity Pass

**[Prototype #307]**: Phase 2 Test Weapons and Supports
_Concept_: Phase 2 should test sword, dagger, spear, axe, mace, bow, crossbow, staff, and wand as basic-attack profiles, with tome and shield represented as support/off-hand style modifiers. The goal is not perfect balance yet; it is to prove that attack weapons and support items create different tactical questions.
_Success Test_: A player can describe why they would choose each weapon in a specific board state.

**[Weapon Profile #308]**: Sword
_Concept_: Sword is the reliable adjacent melee weapon: simple range 1 damage, no trick requirement, good as the baseline comparison point.

**[Weapon Profile #309]**: Dagger
_Concept_: Dagger does not inherently apply bleed. It is a low normal-damage melee weapon that deals much higher damage when the player is Unseen. The desired tuning target is that dagger damage is lower than other same-tier melee weapons when seen, but higher than any other same-tier melee weapon when Unseen; the exact Unseen bonus may need adjustment from the initial 50% proposal depending on damage scale.

**[Weapon Profile #309A]**: Spear
_Concept_: Spear is the reach melee weapon, attacking up to 2 tiles away in a straight line. It should deal less damage than a sword but allow safer positioning, poking over gaps or behind blockers if later rules allow.

**[Weapon Profile #309B]**: Axe
_Concept_: Axe deals a bit less damage than sword but has a percentage chance to apply bleed. Bleed should be defined as a future status effect with clear duration, damage timing, and stacking rules.

**[Weapon Profile #309C]**: Mace
_Concept_: Mace deals a bit less damage than sword but has a percentage chance to disorient. Disorient should be defined as a future status effect, likely reducing enemy accuracy, delaying action, weakening the next attack, or interfering with special abilities.

**[Weapon Profile #310]**: Bow
_Concept_: Bow is the clean ranged weapon: range 4 as a starter value, modest damage, line-of-sight blocked by walls/objects/enemies, and -30% damage against adjacent targets.

**[Weapon Profile #311]**: Crossbow
_Concept_: Crossbow is the short-control ranged weapon: lower range than bow, heavier hit, blocked line of sight, adjacent ranged penalty, and knockback when there is a legal tile behind the target.

**[Weapon Profile #312]**: Staff
_Concept_: Staff is the magical projectile weapon: range 4, blocked by walls/objects/enemies, strong damage at range, but adjacent attacks become melee and deal 50% damage.

**[Weapon Profile #313]**: Wand
_Concept_: Wand is the precision magic weapon: lower damage than staff, but instant targeting that ignores blockers. This makes it good for finishing enemies through clutter and fog-revealed lanes.

**[Weapon Profile #314]**: Tome
_Concept_: Tome does not deal damage on its own by default. It provides stats or modifiers, such as +X% damage from a source, summon chance, magic scaling, curse scaling, or affinity-specific bonuses.

**[Support Profile #314A]**: Shield
_Concept_: Shield provides armor and a percentage chance to block half incoming physical damage. It is an off-hand/support item rather than a basic-attack weapon.

**[Weapon Profile #314B]**: Two-Handed Weapons
_Concept_: Two-handed versions of weapons deal higher damage or stronger effects than one-handed versions, but occupy more equipment capacity and limit off-hand/support options.

**[Prototype #315]**: Weapon Comparison UI
_Concept_: The prototype should show range, damage, targeting style, and special rule for each weapon directly in the UI. The selected weapon panel should explain the tactical role in plain concrete terms.
_Success Test_: The player can swap weapons and understand the expected attack behavior before clicking an enemy.

_Concept_: The generator should carve a guaranteed route from entrance to exit before placing obstacles, hazards, or enemies. This route acts as the validation spine of the level.

**[Generator Rule #318]**: Post-Placement Validation

_Concept_: Phase 3 implementation begins with Small levels only. Medium and Large can be added after the generated Small rooms feel readable and tactical.

### Phase 1 Playtest Script

### Phase 1 Tuning Knobs

- Player HP: too low if the room feels punishing before the player understands it; too high if risk disappears.

- Enemy damage: should create tension without making one mistake fatal in the first prototype.
- Enemy HP: should make weapon choice matter without turning the room into repetition.
- Wall/rubble density: should create positioning problems without causing pathing frustration.
- Caster telegraph delay: should be readable and threatening, not random.
- Ranged penalties: should matter often enough that adjacency creates real danger.

## Tactical Scale, Movement, and Fog of War

**[Scale Decision #299]**: Small Level Average Size
_Concept_: Keep 8x8 as the average size for a Small level or Small combat space. The player may later be able to zoom and scroll across larger current levels, but Small levels should remain compact enough to read on mobile.
_Novelty_: Compact levels stay mobile-friendly while slower movement makes each tile matter.

**[Movement Retune #300]**: Lower Class Movement Baselines
_Concept_: Cut movement roughly in half compared to early brainstorming numbers. Range and magic classes generally start at 3 movement, melee classes generally start at 4 movement, and Ranger is the ranged exception at 4 because mobility is part of the class fantasy.
_Novelty_: Slower movement makes positioning, hazards, line of sight, ranged penalties, and enemy spacing matter much earlier.

**[Movement Target #301]**: Three-Turn Small-Level Crossing
_Concept_: A Small 8x8 level should usually take at least 3 turns of full movement to cross from one side to the other, especially once obstacles, doors, enemies, and hazards are involved.
_Novelty_: Even a small level becomes a tactical commitment rather than a space the player can erase in one turn.

**[Visibility System #302]**: Fog of War
_Concept_: Levels use fog of war. Completely unexplored tiles are black. Explored tiles outside current line of sight are shown under a transparent gray fog, preserving known terrain memory while hiding current live information.
_Novelty_: Exploration becomes a tactical state, supporting scouting, ambushes, secrets, and partial-information choices without real-time pressure.

**[Visibility Rule #303]**: Current Sight Versus Memory
_Concept_: Current line of sight shows live terrain, enemies, hazards, pickups, and objects. Explored-but-unseen fog should show remembered terrain and major static structures, but should not reliably show moving enemies or newly changed hazards unless a special item, talent, or level rule allows it.
_Novelty_: The player can plan from memory but cannot perfectly control unseen space.

**[Visibility Baseline #304]**: Class Line of Sight
_Concept_: All classes start with the same base line-of-sight radius, with 4 tiles as the first prototype value. Talents, items, passives, torches, darkness rules, fog effects, stealth tools, and affinity rules can increase or decrease this later.
_Novelty_: Equal base sight keeps class rules clean. Ranger can still become a better scout through path-preview passives, talents, or gear, while Shadeblade stays special through enemy notice reduction rather than simply seeing farther.

**[Weapon Range Tuning #305]**: Low Starter Ranged Reach
_Concept_: Starter ranged weapon ranges should be fairly low, with 4 tiles as the first draft for bows, staves, and wands. Range can grow through class passives, item affixes, talents, passives, and special weapon identities.
_Novelty_: Shorter starter range makes positioning and LoS matter sooner, while progression can make longer range feel like a real build reward.
