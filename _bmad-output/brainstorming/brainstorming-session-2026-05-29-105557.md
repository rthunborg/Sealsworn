# Brainstorming Session Results

## Session Overview

**Topic:** Narrative spine and MVP story promise for the fantasy turn-based roguelite

**Goals:** Create a story frame that explains why the player enters forward-only procedural levels, why repeated runs and meta progression make sense, why the end bosses matter, and which other MVP-level design areas remain unsettled enough to address before PM/PRD work.

### Context Guidance

This session uses the previous full brainstorming artifact and PM handoff as foundational context. The earlier work already established a strong mechanical identity: mobile-first tactical turn play, seeded forward-only levels, fog of war, weapon-shaped basic attacks, partial information, risk/reward routing, loot/passive rule-bending, limited MVP classes, enemy families, affinities, and meta progression.

The main unresolved area is not content volume. The main unresolved area is the fictional and emotional reason the game exists: what the levels are, why the player pushes forward, what bosses represent, why repeated attempts make sense, and what the first MVP should promise beyond a functional roguelite loop.

### Session Setup

Rasmus wants a new brainstorming session that remains aware of the previous sessions. The focus is story structure and important MVP-level omissions: not every future detail of loot, affinities, enemies, or items, but the missing decisions that could weaken the MVP if left unaddressed.

Recommended initial lens: generate several possible story frames, test each against the existing mechanics, then choose one or two strongest candidates for deeper development.

## Technique Execution Results

**Question Storming:**

**Interactive Focus:** Identify the story questions the MVP must answer so the game feels emotionally coherent, not just mechanically functional.

**Seed Questions Accepted:**

- Why does the hero enter the levels?
- What are the levels, fictionally?
- Why can't the player go back?
- Why do death, repetition, diamonds, and unlocks make sense?
- What are the bosses protecting, causing, or becoming?
- Why should the player care about reaching the end?

**Expanded Question Net:**

1. What is the player trying to save, reclaim, prevent, awaken, seal, or understand?
2. Is the hero an individual protagonist, one of many oathbound delvers, a reincarnating soul, or a chosen class archetype?
3. Is each run a literal journey, a magical attempt, a memory reconstruction, a ritual breach, or a doomed expedition?
4. What makes forward movement mandatory: collapsing levels, sealing doors, ritual law, corruption pressure, pursuit, or oath magic?
5. What makes fog of war fictionally meaningful: darkness, memory loss, cursed perception, unstable reality, or hostile magic?
6. Why are levels different each run: shifting labyrinth, living dungeon, fractured realm, procedural ritual, dream geography, or world wound?
7. Why do Cultists, Undead, and Demons all appear in the same MVP enemy ecology?
8. Are bosses villains, jailers, corrupted guardians, failed heroes, ritual anchors, demon aspects, or seals?
9. What changes when a boss dies beyond "the run ends"?
10. Why can power be found inside the dangerous place instead of brought from outside?
11. What are passives fictionally: blessings, mutations, relic spirits, memories, curses, pacts, divine fragments, or dungeon adaptations?
12. Why can passives be consumed or destroyed, and why would destruction ever matter?
13. What are diamonds fictionally: soul shards, oath fragments, memory crystals, demon glass, star-metal, or sanctuary currency?
14. Who or what receives knowledge and resources after a failed run?
15. What is the hub or between-run home: sanctuary, camp, guild hall, ruined chapel, archive, dream shore, or sealed gate?
16. Why do meta unlocks expand possibility rather than simply make the hero stronger?
17. What is the emotional promise: desperate descent, clever dungeon-reading, forbidden power fantasy, heroic purification, occult bargain, or survival pilgrimage?
18. How much story should appear during play: opening premise only, sparse run events, codex discoveries, boss lines, or hub progression?
19. What must the first MVP ending communicate, even if it uses placeholder art and limited content?
20. What question should the player want answered after their first death?

**Question Storming Breakthrough:** The story frame must explain not only the setting, but also the rules of repetition, forward-only movement, reward mutation, and finale stakes. A generic dungeon crawl premise is probably too weak unless it specifically supports those mechanics.

**Mythic Frameworks:**

**Interactive Focus:** Generate story frames that make the existing mechanics feel inevitable rather than decorative.

**[Story Frame #1]**: The Failing Sealed Labyrinth
_Concept_: The game takes place in a moving labyrinth originally built as a deterrent and containment structure around a Cthulhu-like being. Mad cultists have already broken the outer seals, and the imprisoned being is slowly breaking free from the shackles that bind it; the hero must push through the shifting containment layers and end the threat before it creates hell on earth.
_Novelty_: The labyrinth's procedural movement, forward-only pressure, fog, dangerous bargains, and finale all become expressions of a failing prison rather than arbitrary roguelite conventions.

**[World Model #2]**: General Containment Place
_Concept_: The labyrinth is not built only for the final entity. It is a general prison for lesser evils, failed cult experiments, corrupted guardians, demons, undead, and sealed minibosses encountered on the way to the core.
_Novelty_: This gives the MVP enemy families a shared ecology: Cultists are intruders and seal-breakers, Undead are failed delvers or ancient prisoners, and Demons are leakage from deeper containment layers.

**[Stakes #3]**: The Breach Clock
_Concept_: The cultists have already done enough damage that failure is not a distant possibility; it is now a countdown. Each run is an urgent attempt to reach deeper before the final being fully escapes.
_Novelty_: This creates story urgency without needing real-time pressure during combat, matching the game's "think forever, act carefully" pillar.

**[Boss Meaning #4]**: Shackle-Knots and Prison Kings
_Concept_: Bosses and minibosses can represent different failed parts of the containment system: broken wardens, imprisoned lesser gods, cult champions, demon anchors, or shackle-knots that must be destroyed before the core can be reached. Defeating them changes the prison's state and explains progression unlocks.
_Novelty_: Bosses are not just final stat checks; they are meaningful pieces of the containment architecture.

**[Run Logic #5]**: Each Descent Is a Breach Attempt
_Concept_: A run is a sanctioned descent from a sanctuary or breach camp into the living prison. The labyrinth seals behind the hero, preventing backtracking, because its containment law allows only inward movement once a breach path opens.
_Novelty_: No-backtracking becomes an in-world rule with emotional weight: every door is a commitment deeper into the prison.

**[Story Revelation #6]**: False Final Victory
_Concept_: The heroes initially believe the core being can be killed. After the first apparent victory, they discover the being is truly immortal and has retreated deeper into the containment labyrinth instead of dying.
_Novelty_: The first win becomes a story twist that justifies post-win expansion rather than making continued play feel non-canonical.

**[Endgame Structure #7]**: Immortal Retreat
_Concept_: When defeated, the being withdraws closer to its other-dimensional portal, where it becomes stronger and more dangerous. Later runs push toward deeper containment layers, harder bosses, altered rules, and more desperate forms of banishment.
_Novelty_: Difficulty escalation and deeper endgame modes become the being's defensive retreat rather than arbitrary scaling.

**[Victory Model #8]**: Temporary Banishment as Victory
_Concept_: The ultimate achievable victory, at least for now, is not permanent destruction but temporary banishment back through the portal. The heroes can delay catastrophe, force the being back, and buy the world time.
_Novelty_: Victory remains satisfying while preserving long-term roguelite replay: the threat can return, but each successful banishment matters.

**[Postgame Logic #9]**: Ever-Vigilant Wardens
_Concept_: After defeating the final boss, the heroes understand that vigilance is now their duty. New runs continue because the being may return, lesser prisoners may exploit weakened seals, and the method for recreating the permanent seal is still unknown.
_Novelty_: Continued play after the final boss becomes part of the story instead of a non-diegetic "new game plus" convention.

**[World Architecture #10]**: Portal-Spawn Containment Architecture
_Concept_: The containment labyrinth was built around the only known point where the being can enter the world: an other-dimensional portal or spawn point. The labyrinth's purpose is to delay, confuse, weaken, and prevent the being from reaching the outer world.
_Novelty_: The moving labyrinth is not random magic architecture; it is a hostile containment machine designed around a cosmic breach point.

**[Hero Frame #11]**: Descendants of the Forgotten Order
_Concept_: The playable heroes are descendants of an old forgotten order sworn to maintain the Labyrinth. They may have lived ordinary lives until the seals broke and their ancestral duty violently reawakened.
_Novelty_: The heroes are not random treasure seekers or generic chosen ones; they inherit a responsibility their world forgot.

**[Soul Rule #12]**: Broken Spiritual Seal
_Concept_: When the cultists broke the Labyrinth's seals, they also broke a dormant spiritual seal carried within the descendants of the old order. This awakened their ancestral bond to the Labyrinth and made escape from the duty impossible.
_Novelty_: The call to adventure is not a prophecy invitation; it is a binding, frightening inheritance.

**[Memory System #13]**: Ancestral Memory Flood
_Concept_: The broken spiritual seal floods the descendants with memories from their ancestors: fragments of old combat disciplines, containment lore, class identities, monster knowledge, and glimpses of failed descents. This explains why knowledge, classes, codex entries, and mastery can unlock over time.
_Novelty_: Meta progression becomes remembrance rather than grind.

**[Death Loop #14]**: Soulbound Outpost
_Concept_: The descendants' spirits are bound to an outpost at the Labyrinth entrance. When they die inside the Labyrinth, their spirits return to the outpost instead of passing into the afterlife.
_Novelty_: Repeated runs become a curse of duty with clear in-world rules, giving death continuity without erasing consequence.

**[Progression Logic #15]**: Diegetic Meta Progression
_Concept_: Each failed attempt still matters because the hero's spirit retains memories, discoveries, and recovered fragments of the old order's knowledge. Diamonds or another meta currency can represent condensed memory, seal fragments, or awakened oath-shards used to rebuild lost capabilities.
_Novelty_: Permanent progression is not an abstract account layer; it is the outpost and the descendants slowly remembering how to fight a cosmic containment war.

**Emerging Preferred Story Spine:**

The Labyrinth was not built to keep heroes out. It was built to keep something in.

The Labyrinth is a moving containment machine built around the only known portal where an immortal Cthulhu-like being can enter the world. The old order that maintained the prison has been forgotten, but its descendants still carry dormant spiritual seals. When cultists break the Labyrinth's outer seals, they also awaken those seals inside the descendants, flooding them with ancestral memories and binding their spirits to the last outpost at the Labyrinth entrance.

The heroes descend because the being is already breaking free. Death returns their spirits to the outpost instead of the afterlife, and each failed attempt recovers more memory, knowledge, and lost capability. The first apparent victory reveals that the being cannot truly be killed; it can only be driven deeper or temporarily banished. Long-term progression becomes the effort to rediscover how to recreate the permanent seal.

**[Hub Frame #16]**: Last Outpost
_Concept_: The outpost is the last or final outpost of the forgotten order, built at the Labyrinth entrance. It serves as the physical home for class selection, run start, meta progression, recovery, and visible reconstruction over time.
_Novelty_: The hub is not a menu wrapper; it is the last surviving piece of the containment order's infrastructure.

**[Hub System #17]**: Memory Archive Refuge
_Concept_: Beneath or within the keep is a memory archive where ancestral echoes, recovered fragments, codex entries, class mastery, and seal knowledge awaken between runs. The player's spirit returns here after death, carrying fragments of what they learned inside.
_Novelty_: The codex and meta progression screens become places in the world rather than disconnected UI tabs.

**[Faction Frame #18]**: Dream-Infected Liberation Cult
_Concept_: The cultists believe the being in the Labyrinth is a god unjustly imprisoned by fearful mortals. In reality, the being has been whispering through dreams, visions, inherited madness, and false revelations, gradually shaping their beliefs.
_Novelty_: The cultists are both responsible and manipulated, giving them more texture than simple evil worshippers.

**[Cult Motive #19]**: Mercy Through Release
_Concept_: Cultists frame the broken seals as an act of mercy, liberation, or cosmic justice. They may believe the world must be remade, that death is a prison, or that the being's arrival will dissolve suffering into a higher truth.
_Novelty_: The cult's rhetoric can sound almost compassionate, which makes their catastrophic actions more unsettling.

**[MVP Finale #20]**: Larval Avatar Finale
_Concept_: The MVP final boss is a forming body or larval avatar used by the immortal being to enter the world. Defeating it appears to kill the being, but the post-victory reveal shows that the true entity has only retreated deeper toward the portal.
_Novelty_: The first final boss provides a satisfying victory while preserving the long-term truth that the being cannot be permanently killed by ordinary means.

**[Boss Roadmap #21]**: First Sealbreaker as Cult Boss
_Concept_: The cult leader who shattered the outer seals can serve as a major cult boss before or alongside the MVP finale. This enemy embodies the dream-infected liberation ideology and gives the cult plot a face.
_Novelty_: The cult's role in the catastrophe becomes personal without replacing the cosmic threat.

**[Boss Roadmap #22]**: Broken Warden as Future Tragic Boss
_Concept_: A later boss can be an ancient guardian or warden corrupted by centuries of proximity to the core. This boss reveals that even the old order was damaged by the thing it contained.
_Novelty_: The descendants may have to fight their own inheritance, complicating the idea that the old order was purely heroic.

**[Narrative Delivery #23]**: Story as Optional Discovery
_Concept_: The story should unfold across runs through mystery, fragments, codex discoveries, environmental hints, boss implications, class memories, and ambiguous revelations. Players who care can piece together the truth, while players who ignore lore can still enjoy the tactical roguelite loop.
_Novelty_: Narrative becomes a reward layer rather than a gate to understanding or enjoying the game.

**[Onboarding Rule #24]**: One-Paragraph Intro Rule
_Concept_: The first intro should be extremely short, likely a single paragraph or equivalent, giving only enough premise to orient the player: the Labyrinth is failing, something inside is waking, and the hero is bound to descend. Deeper explanations should come later through play.
_Novelty_: The game starts with immediate momentum and preserves mystery instead of front-loading lore.

**[UX Narrative Rule #25]**: Skippable Control Loss
_Concept_: Any moment that removes player control, such as a cutscene, intro, boss reveal, death sequence, or post-victory scene, should be skippable. The story can invite attention but should never hold the player's time hostage.
_Novelty_: This protects replayability and respects players who primarily want the game loop.

**[Tone Principle #26]**: Ambiguity by Design
_Concept_: Some story elements should remain intentionally unclear, contradictory, or incomplete. The old order, the cult's dreams, the being's nature, the true cost of sealing it, and the reliability of ancestral memories can all retain mystery.
_Novelty_: Ambiguity supports cosmic horror and long-term discovery while avoiding over-explaining the threat into something mundane.

**[Tone Boundary #27]**: Dark Medieval Baseline
_Concept_: The general world sits at classic dark medieval fantasy technology: steel, wood, stone keeps, candles, scrolls, armor, bows, old churches, ruined roads, and fearful villages. Ordinary people do not understand the Labyrinth as technology; they experience it as cursed architecture, divine punishment, or forbidden old magic.
_Novelty_: The world stays grounded in fantasy, making the ancient containment systems feel rare and uncanny instead of turning the setting into broad science fantasy.

**[Ancient Layer #28]**: Wardenwork Relic-Technology
_Concept_: The Labyrinth contains ancient forgotten containment mechanisms built by or through the old order: seal engines, memory vaults, impossible doors, light-lances, stasis chambers, mirrored identity locks, and portal shackles. These are perceived as holy relics, black glass machinery, rune-metal instruments, or impossible magic rather than modern technology.
_Novelty_: Ancient technology becomes mythic infrastructure: it can support cosmic containment without breaking the medieval fantasy surface.

**[Passive Fiction #29]**: Passives as Awakened Memories
_Concept_: Passive pickups can represent awakened ancestral memories, fighting instincts, old oath techniques, forbidden warden knowledge, or echoes recovered from failed descendants. Consuming a passive means integrating the memory into the current hero's spirit and body.
_Novelty_: Build mutation becomes remembrance: the player grows stranger and stronger by recovering what the bloodline forgot.

**[Passive Choice #30]**: Consume or Destroy Memory Choice
_Concept_: Destroying a passive can mean rejecting, purging, sealing, or breaking an awakened memory before it fully binds to the hero. This can produce smaller benefits such as Oath Shards, Echoes, purification, curse reduction, or triggering hidden refusal paths.
_Novelty_: The signature Consume/Destroy choice gains emotional meaning: not every inherited memory deserves to live again.

**[Affinity Fiction #31]**: Affinities as Failed Safety Measures
_Concept_: Level affinities are not simply elemental themes; they are containment protocols malfunctioning, leaking, overcorrecting, or being corrupted by the being's pressure. Scorched may be a purge protocol, Frozen a stasis protocol, Darkness a concealment protocol, Mirrored an identity-confusion protocol, Cursed a broken oath-law, and Flooded/Conductive a failing ward-conduit system.
_Novelty_: Affinities become the Labyrinth's damaged immune system, tying level variety directly to the story.

**[Rare Reward #32]**: Rare Impossible Technology Rewards
_Concept_: Ultra-rare rewards can expose pieces of ancient forgotten technology, such as a beam-like warden relic that fires a line of containment light. These should feel like sacred or alien artifacts with limited availability, not common firearms or a new tech tier.
_Novelty_: The game can occasionally surprise players with impossible relics while preserving the dark medieval baseline.

**Mythic Frameworks Current Takeaways:**

- The preferred story spine is the failing containment Labyrinth, not a generic dungeon crawl.
- The world baseline should remain dark medieval fantasy.
- The ancient containment layer should feel like forgotten Wardenwork: relic-magic, black glass machinery, rune-metal, seal engines, impossible doors, memory vaults, and rare light-based relics.
- Passives are best framed as awakened memories, old oath techniques, forbidden instincts, or ancestral echoes.
- Affinities are best framed as failed safety measures and malfunctioning containment protocols.
- Story should unfold through optional discovery, not forced exposition.
- MVP should keep the story frame strong but narrow: short intro, one hub frame, one major cult motive, one final boss reveal, limited affinities, and enough lore hooks to make the run meaningful.

**Six Thinking Hats:**

**Interactive Focus:** Stress-test the preferred story spine for MVP usefulness, player emotion, risks, scope, and next decisions.

**White Hat - Facts and Constraints:**

**[MVP Truth #33]**: Game Loop First
_Concept_: The story only works for MVP if the player experiences a dopamine-filled and exciting game loop that makes them want to explore, progress, and start another run. Narrative should support that loop through motivation, mystery, reward meaning, and atmosphere, but the game must remain fun for players who skip or ignore lore.
_Novelty_: This keeps the story spine from becoming scope-heavy exposition and anchors it to the roguelite's core promise.

**Red Hat - Desired Emotional Run Feel:**

**[Emotional Target #34]**: Clever, Lucky, Tense, Curious
_Concept_: During a good run, the player should feel clever for creating a strong synergy or build, excited that more of the story was uncovered, amazed by fortunate run-specific luck, and tense because they do not want to ruin the run before the payoff of a risky decision arrives.
_Novelty_: The emotional center is not just power growth; it is the combination of self-authored cleverness, mystery reward, lucky discovery, and fear of losing a promising setup.

**[Emotional Loop #35]**: Delayed Risk Payoff
_Concept_: Risky choices should often create a period of suspense before paying off: accepting a dangerous passive, choosing a cursed route, delaying a reward, or preserving a strange object can make the player think "this could become amazing if I survive long enough."
_Novelty_: Tension comes from protecting a future possibility, not only avoiding immediate death.

**Yellow Hat - Benefits of the Current Story Spine:**

**[Story Benefit #36]**: Mechanics Gain Meaning
_Concept_: The failing containment Labyrinth explains roguelite repetition, forward-only movement, procedural shifting, meta progression, bosses, affinities, passives, and post-victory runs. Existing mechanics feel like consequences of the world rather than disconnected genre conventions.
_Novelty_: The story does not sit on top of the design; it turns the design into fiction.

**[Story Benefit #37]**: Mystery Without Mandatory Reading
_Concept_: The old order, ancestral memories, cult dreams, Wardenwork, and immortal being create many optional discovery hooks. Players who enjoy lore can investigate, while players who skip text still get a coherent tactical roguelite.
_Novelty_: The story can deepen across runs without becoming homework.

**[Story Benefit #38]**: Scalable Endgame
_Concept_: The immortal retreat and temporary banishment model supports first victory, post-win expansion, harder layers, alternate bosses, and long-term vigilance. Continued play remains canonical because the threat can be delayed but not easily ended.
_Novelty_: The endgame can grow without invalidating the MVP finale.

**Black Hat - Risks and Guardrails:**

**[Risk #39]**: Wardenwork Becoming Too Sci-Fi
_Concept_: Ancient containment technology could break the dark medieval fantasy tone if it reads as modern devices, clean machinery, guns, terminals, or science-fiction infrastructure. Wardenwork should instead be framed as relic-magic: rune-metal, black glass, sealed light, impossible stone, oath engines, saintlike instruments, and half-understood containment rites.
_Novelty_: The world can include ancient technology without looking or feeling like a sci-fi setting.

**[Guardrail #40]**: No Common Tech Layer
_Concept_: Ordinary civilization remains classic fantasy level, and Wardenwork appears rarely as ancient, sacred, alien, or cursed artifacts. Even ultra-rare "beam" rewards should be named and presented as light-lances, seal focuses, sun-spears, or containment relics rather than guns.
_Novelty_: Rare technological wonder becomes more powerful because it is exceptional.

**[Risk #41]**: Passive Memory Repetition
_Concept_: If every passive is presented plainly as an "awakened memory," the reward pool may feel repetitive and flavorless. Passives need distinct names, tones, and story identities connected to the game universe.
_Novelty_: "Awakened memories" should be the metaphysical category, not the visible naming formula.

**[Guardrail #42]**: Passives Need Authored Identity
_Concept_: Each passive should have a specific evocative identity such as an oath, scar, technique, relic echo, forbidden memory, warden instinct, cult-tainted dream, broken protocol, ancestor's failure, or seal fragment. Names and flavor should imply story while the mechanical text remains concrete.
_Novelty_: Passive rewards become miniature lore discoveries and build choices at the same time.

**Green Hat - Creative Improvements:**

**[Narrative Moment #43]**: First Death Memory
_Concept_: After the player's first death, a very short ancestral line appears, such as "Good. You remembered how to die." This reveals that death return is part of the old order's emergency system without requiring a long explanation.
_Novelty_: A single line turns the first failure into mystery, tone, and motivation.

**[Hub UX #44]**: Outpost Rooms as UI
_Concept_: Between-run systems can map to places in the ruined keep: the Codex is the Archive, class mastery is the Hall of Oaths, meta upgrades are the Seal Table, and run launch happens at the Gate or Descent Stair.
_Novelty_: The hub can support progression and menus without requiring a large explorable town in MVP.

**[Affinity Presentation #45]**: Protocol-Language Affinities
_Concept_: Affinity names can stay readable, but previews and flavor can reveal containment language. A Scorched level might be "Purge Fires Active," Darkness might be "Veil Failure," and Frozen might be "Stasis Breach."
_Novelty_: The player learns that level modifiers are failed safety systems through repeated play, not exposition.

**[Reward UI #46]**: Passive Flavor Template
_Concept_: Passive reward modals should include an evocative name, one short flavor line, exact mechanical effects, and clear Consume/Destroy choices. Flavor creates story discovery while mechanics remain readable and unambiguous.
_Novelty_: The signature passive choice can serve both buildcraft and lore without slowing players down.

**[Victory Reveal #47]**: First Victory Twist Line
_Concept_: After defeating the Larval Avatar, the game can deliver one sharp reveal line, such as "It did not die. It learned the way back." This reframes victory as temporary banishment and sets up post-win runs.
_Novelty_: The twist can land in seconds while preserving mystery and replay motivation.
